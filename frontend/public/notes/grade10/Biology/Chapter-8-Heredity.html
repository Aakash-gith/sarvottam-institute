Introduction to Heredity

Heredity is the transmission of genetic information from parents to offspring. It explains why children resemble their parents and how traits pass through generations.

Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

Gregor Mendel discovered the laws of inheritance through pea plant experiments.

1. Law of Segregation (First Law)

Statement: Factors (genes) for a trait segregate during gamete formation, and each gamete receives only one factor.
  • Traits are controlled by pairs of factors (alleles)
  • During meiosis, allele pairs separate
  • Each gamete gets one allele
  • Offspring receive one allele from each parent

2. Law of Independent Assortment (Second Law)

Alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation. A dihybrid cross shows this: RrYy produces nine different phenotypes in 9:3:3:1 ratio.

3. Law of Dominance

When two contrasting alleles are present, the dominant allele expresses and masks the recessive allele.

Monohybrid Cross

A cross between organisms differing in one trait.

Example: RR × rr (Red flower × White flower)
  • P generation: RR (red) × rr (white)
  • F₁ generation: All Rr (red flowers) - shows Law of Dominance
  • F₂ generation: 1RR : 2Rr : 1rr = 3 red : 1 white (3:1 ratio)

Dihybrid Cross

A cross between organisms differing in two traits.

Example: RRYY × rryy (Red, Round × White, Wrinkled)
  • P generation: RRYY × rryy
  • F₁ generation: All RrYy (Red, Round)
  • F₂ generation: 9 Red Round : 3 Red Wrinkled : 3 White Round : 1 White Wrinkled (9:3:3:1)
This demonstrates Law of Independent Assortment

Punnett Square

A tool to predict offspring genotypes and phenotypes.

  • Rows: Alleles from one parent
  • Columns: Alleles from other parent
  • Cells: Possible offspring genotypes
  • 4-box grid for monohybrid, 16-box for dihybrid

Chromosomal Basis of Inheritance

Genes are located on chromosomes. During meiosis, chromosomes separate, distributing genes to gametes.

  • Chromosome: Structure containing genes, made of DNA and proteins
  • Gene Location: Specific position on chromosome (locus)
  • Homologous Chromosomes: Carry alleles of same genes
  • Diploid (2n): Full set of chromosomes (paired)
  • Haploid (n): Single set of chromosomes (in gametes)

Human Genetics

Chromosome Number

  • Total Chromosomes: 46 (23 pairs)
  • Autosomes: 22 pairs (non-sex chromosomes)
  • Sex Chromosomes: 1 pair (XX or XY)
  • Male: 44 + XY
  • Female: 44 + XX

X-linked Traits

Traits controlled by genes on X chromosome.

  • Example: Color blindness (recessive)
  • Male (XcY): Color blind (if one Xc)
  • Female (XCXc): Carrier (normal but carries gene)
  • Female (XcXc): Color blind (rare)

Genetic Disorders

  • Albinism: Lack of melanin, recessive trait
  • Cystic Fibrosis: Autosomal recessive disorder
  • Hemophilia: X-linked recessive bleeding disorder
  • Color Blindness: X-linked recessive trait
  • Huntington's Disease: Autosomal dominant neurological disorder

Mutations

Permanent changes in DNA sequence that can affect traits.

  • Point Mutation: Change in single nucleotide
  • Insertion: Addition of nucleotides
  • Deletion: Loss of nucleotides
  • Duplication: Repetition of nucleotides
  • Inversion: Reversal of nucleotide sequence
  • Causes: Radiation, chemicals, viral infection, spontaneous errors

Evolution

Evolution is change in heritable traits of populations over generations.

Evidence of Evolution

  • Fossil Records: Show gradual changes over time
  • Homologous Structures: Similar structures in different species (bat wing, human arm, whale flipper)
  • Analogous Structures: Similar function, different origin (bird wing, butterfly wing)
  • Vestigial Structures: Reduced/non-functional remnants (tailbone in humans, wings in ostriches)
  • DNA Similarity: Genetic similarity among organisms, especially between related species

Natural Selection

Darwin's Theory: Organisms with advantageous traits survive better and reproduce more, passing traits to offspring.
  • Variation in populations
  • Struggle for existence
  • Survival of fittest
  • Gradual accumulation of advantageous traits

Speciation

Formation of new species from existing species through accumulated genetic changes.

  • Isolation: Geographic or reproductive isolation
  • Variation: Different mutations in isolated populations
  • Natural Selection: Different selective pressures in different environments
  • New Species: Eventually cannot interbreed with original species

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Genes control traits; alleles are gene variants
  • ✓ Dominant alleles mask recessive in heterozygotes
  • ✓ Mendel's laws: Segregation, Independent Assortment, Dominance
  • ✓ Genotype: genetic makeup; Phenotype: observable traits
  • ✓ Monohybrid 3:1 ratio; Dihybrid 9:3:3:1 ratio
  • ✓ Humans: 46 chromosomes, 23 pairs
  • ✓ Evolution: Change in populations over time via natural selection
