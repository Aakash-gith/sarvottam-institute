Carbon - The Versatile Element

Carbon (C, atomic number 6, atomic mass 12) is the second most abundant element in the human body and forms the backbone of all living organisms and organic compounds. Its unique property is its ability to form four covalent bonds.

Allotropes of Carbon

Allotropes are different forms of the same element with different structures and properties.

  • Diamond: Hardest form of carbon, used in cutting tools and jewelry. Each carbon is bonded to 4 other carbons in a tetrahedral arrangement.
  • Graphite: Soft and layered form, conducts electricity, used in pencils and electrodes. Each carbon bonded to 3 others in a planar hexagonal structure.
  • Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀): Football-shaped molecule, discovered in 1985, has unique electronic properties
  • Amorphous Carbon: Non-crystalline form including coal, charcoal, soot, and coke

Properties of Carbon

  • Valency: 4 (forms 4 covalent bonds)
  • Electron Configuration: 2, 4
  • Non-metallic nature: Does not conduct electricity (except graphite)
  • Catenation: Ability to form long chains with itself
  • Tetravalency: Can form single, double, and triple bonds

Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are compounds that contain carbon and are typically compounds of carbon with hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, or other elements. Over 10 million organic compounds are known.

Classification of Organic Compounds

  • Hydrocarbons: Compounds containing only C and H
    • Alkanes (C_nH₂ₙ₊₂): Saturated hydrocarbons with only single bonds (Methane, Ethane, Propane)
    • Alkenes (C_nH₂ₙ): Unsaturated hydrocarbons with one double bond (Ethene, Propene)
    • Alkynes (C_nH₂ₙ₋₂): Unsaturated hydrocarbons with triple bond (Ethyne/Acetylene)
  • Oxygenated Compounds: Contain oxygen (-OH, C=O groups)
    • Alcohols: Contain -OH group (Ethanol, Methanol)
    • Aldehydes: Contain -CHO group
    • Ketones: Contain C=O group
    • Carboxylic Acids: Contain -COOH group

Functional Groups

Functional groups are atoms or groups of atoms bonded in a characteristic manner that define the structure and reactivity of organic compounds.

  • Hydroxyl Group (-OH): Found in alcohols and carboxylic acids
  • Carbonyl Group (C=O): Found in aldehydes and ketones
  • Carboxyl Group (-COOH): Found in carboxylic acids (acids)
  • Ester Group (-COOR): Found in esters
  • Ether Group (-O-): Found in ethers

Homologous Series

A homologous series is a group of organic compounds with the same functional group, successive members differing by CH₂ units, showing gradual change in physical properties and similar chemical properties.

Examples:
Alkane Series:
Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀)

Alkene Series:
Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆), Butene (C₄H₈)

Characteristics:
• General formula represents all members
• Molecular mass increases by 14 for each successive member (alkanes)
• Physical properties change gradually
• Chemical properties similar within series

Nomenclature of Organic Compounds (IUPAC)

IUPAC system provides a systematic name to organic compounds based on their structure.

  • Prefixes for carbon number: Meth-(1), Eth-(2), Prop-(3), But-(4), Pent-(5), Hex-(6)
  • Suffixes: -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -yne (alkyne), -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -oic acid (carboxylic acid)

Isomerism

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements. They show different physical and chemical properties.

Types of Isomerism:
• Structural Isomerism: Different arrangements of atoms
• Stereoisomerism: Different 3D arrangements

Example:
Butane (C₄H₁₀) has 2 isomers:
1. n-Butane (straight chain)
2. Isobutane/2-methylpropane (branched)

Combustion of Carbon Compounds

Complete combustion of organic compounds produces carbon dioxide and water, releasing energy.

C_nH_m + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + Energy

Example: C₂H₆ + 3.5O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Carbon has multiple allotropes
  • ✓ Organic compounds contain carbon
  • ✓ Functional groups determine compound properties
  • ✓ Homologous series show gradual property change
