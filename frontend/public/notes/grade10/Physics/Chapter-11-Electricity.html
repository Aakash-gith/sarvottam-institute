Electric Charge

Electric charge is the physical property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electromagnetic field. It is measured in Coulombs (C).

Types of Electric Charge

  • Positive Charge: Produced by loss of electrons (deficiency)
  • Negative Charge: Produced by gain of electrons (excess)
  • Elementary Charge (e): 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (charge on one electron/proton)

Coulomb's Law

F = k(q₁q₂)/r²
where k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C², q₁, q₂ are charges, r is distance

OR:
F = (q₁q₂)/(4πε₀r²)
where ε₀ = permittivity of free space

Electric Field and Electric Potential

  • Electric Field (E): Force per unit charge. E = F/q
  • Electric Potential (V): Energy per unit charge. V = W/q
  • Potential Difference: Difference in potential between two points: V = V₁ - V₂
  • Unit: Volt (V) or J/C

Electric Current

Electric current is the flow of electric charge in a conductor.

I = Q/t
where I = current (Amperes), Q = charge (Coulombs), t = time (seconds)

Direction: Conventional current flows from positive to negative terminal
Actual flow: Electrons flow from negative to positive terminal

Ohm's Law

At constant temperature, current through a conductor is directly proportional to potential difference and inversely proportional to resistance.

V = IR
where V = potential difference (Volts), I = current (Amperes), R = resistance (Ohms - Ω)

Resistance: R = ρl/A
where ρ = resistivity, l = length, A = cross-sectional area

Resistivity and Conductivity

  • Resistivity (ρ): Property of material that opposes current flow. Measured in Ω·m
  • Conductivity (σ): Ability of material to conduct current. σ = 1/ρ
  • Good Conductors: Low resistivity (Copper: 1.7×10⁻⁸ Ω·m)
  • Good Insulators: High resistivity (Glass: 10¹¹ Ω·m)

Effect of Temperature on Resistance

Rₜ = R₀(1 + αΔT)
where Rₜ = resistance at temperature T, R₀ = resistance at 0°C, α = temperature coefficient

Electrical Power and Energy

Power:
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Unit: Watt (W) = Joule/second

Electrical Energy:
W = Pt = VIt
Unit: Joule (J) or Kilowatt-hour (kWh)
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

Joule's Law of Heating

When current flows through a conductor, heat is produced.

H = I²Rt
where H = heat produced (Joules), I = current, R = resistance, t = time

Also: H = Pt = VIt

Combination of Resistances

  • Series Connection: Resistances connected one after another
    • Total Resistance: R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
    • Same current through all resistors
    • Voltages add up
  • Parallel Connection: Resistances connected side by side
    • Total Resistance: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
    • Same voltage across all resistors
    • Currents add up

Electromotive Force (EMF)

EMF is the total energy per unit charge provided by a battery or power source.

ε = E/Q
where ε = EMF, E = total energy, Q = charge

Terminal Voltage:
V = ε - Ir
where r = internal resistance of battery

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ I = Q/t (Current)
  • ✓ V = IR (Ohm's Law)
  • ✓ P = VI = I²R (Power)
  • ✓ H = I²Rt (Joule's Law)
  • ✓ Series: R_total = R₁ + R₂
  • ✓ Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
