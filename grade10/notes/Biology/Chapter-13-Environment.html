Introduction to Ecology

Ecology is the study of organisms and their interactions with each other and the physical environment. Understanding ecosystems helps us manage resources and protect biodiversity.

Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with the physical (abiotic) environment.

Components of Ecosystem:
  • Abiotic (Non-living): Sunlight, temperature, water, soil, air, pH
  • Biotic (Living): Producers, consumers, decomposers

Types of Organisms

Category Role Examples
Producers Autotrophs: Synthesize food using sunlight Green plants, algae
Primary Consumers Herbivores: Feed on producers Grasshoppers, deer, rabbits
Secondary Consumers Carnivores/Omnivores: Feed on primary consumers Frogs, snakes, small birds
Tertiary Consumers Top carnivores: Feed on secondary consumers Eagles, lions, humans
Decomposers Break down dead organisms, return nutrients Bacteria, fungi

Food Web

Multiple interconnected food chains showing complex feeding relationships.

Characteristics:
  • Shows all possible food chains in an ecosystem
  • One organism may eat multiple others
  • More realistic than food chain
  • More stable due to multiple pathways
  • One organism may occupy multiple trophic levels

Trophic Levels and Energy Transfer

  • 1st Trophic Level: Producers (Plants) - receive solar energy
  • 2nd Trophic Level: Primary consumers (Herbivores)
  • 3rd Trophic Level: Secondary consumers (Carnivores)
  • 4th Trophic Level: Tertiary consumers (Top predators)
10% Law (Energy Loss):
  • Only ~10% of energy transfers to next trophic level
  • 90% lost as heat during respiration, movement, etc.
  • Energy decreases at each level
  • Limits number of trophic levels (usually 3-4)

Ecological Pyramids

Pyramid of Numbers

Shows number of organisms at each trophic level.

  • Usually pyramidal (more producers than consumers)
  • Exception: One tree (producer) supporting many insects

Pyramid of Biomass

Shows total biomass (dry weight) at each trophic level.

  • Generally pyramidal shape
  • Biomass decreases upward
  • Exception: Aquatic ecosystems (inverted due to small but numerous phytoplankton)

Pyramid of Energy

Shows energy available at each trophic level.

  • Always pyramidal
  • Follows 10% law
  • Most useful representation

  • Transpiration: Water released by plants
  • Condensation: Water vapor forms clouds
  • Precipitation: Rain, snow returns water to earth
  • Infiltration: Water soaks into soil (groundwater)
  • Runoff: Water flows to rivers and oceans

Biodiversity

Variety of all living organisms, their genetic variation, and ecosystems they inhabit.

Levels of Biodiversity

  • Genetic Diversity: Variation within species (different alleles)
  • Species Diversity: Different species in an area
  • Ecosystem Diversity: Different habitats and ecosystems

Conservation of Biodiversity

Why Conserve Biodiversity?

  • Aesthetic value (beautiful organisms)
  • Medicinal value (drugs from plants and animals)
  • Ecological value (ecosystem balance)
  • Economic value (food, timber, tourism)
  • Ethical responsibility to future generations

Conservation Strategies

  • Protected Areas: National parks, sanctuaries, biosphere reserves
  • Legal Protection: Laws against poaching, habitat destruction
  • Breeding Programs: Captive breeding for endangered species
  • Habitat Restoration: Restoration of damaged ecosystems
  • Education: Public awareness about conservation
  • Sustainable Use: Controlled harvesting of resources

Environmental Issues

Pollution

  • Air Pollution: Gases and particles from industries, vehicles, fossil fuels
  • Water Pollution: Industrial waste, agricultural runoff, plastic
  • Soil Pollution: Pesticides, heavy metals, industrial waste
  • Noise Pollution: Excessive noise from traffic, industries

Non-Biodegradable Waste

  • Plastic: Takes 500+ years to decompose
  • Glass: Takes 1 million years to decompose
  • Problem: Accumulates in environment, harms wildlife
  • Solution: Reduce use, recycle, biodegradable alternatives

Ozone Depletion

  • Cause: CFC (chlorofluorocarbon) emissions
  • Effect: Reduces UV protection, increases skin cancer risk
  • Solution: Montreal Protocol banned CFCs

  • Effect: Temperature rise, sea level rise, extreme weather
  • Impact: Habitat loss, species extinction, agriculture affected
  • Solution: Renewable energy, reduced emissions, reforestation

Deforestation

  • Cause: Logging, agriculture, urbanization
  • Effect: Habitat loss, COâ‚‚ increase, soil erosion
  • Impact: Species extinction, climate change, reduced rainfall
  • Solution: Afforestation, protected areas, sustainable forestry

Sustainable Development

Meeting present needs without compromising ability of future generations to meet their needs.

Principles:
  • Conservation of resources
  • Renewable energy use
  • Waste reduction and recycling
  • Organic farming practices
  • Protected habitat areas
  • Community participation in conservation

Individual Actions for Environment Protection

  • Reduce, reuse, recycle
  • Use public transport, carpool, or cycle
  • Conserve water and electricity
  • Plant trees, maintain gardens
  • Avoid single-use plastics
  • Support sustainable products
  • Educate others about conservation
  • Minimize pesticide/chemical use
