5.1 Nutrition

Nutrition

The process by which an organism obtains its food.

Modes of Nutrition

1. Autotrophic

Organisms synthesize their own food from inorganic materials (CO₂, H₂O) using sunlight.
Ex: Green Plants.

2. Heterotrophic

Organisms depend on others for food. Cannot synthesize their own.
Ex: Animals, Fungi.

Autotrophic Nutrition (Photosynthesis)

Process of converting simple inorganic material into complex high-energy molecules (Carbohydrates).

6CO₂ + 12H₂O Sunlight/Chlorophyll C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ + 6H₂O

Raw Materials

  • Sunlight: Source of energy.
  • Chlorophyll: Absorbs light energy.
  • CO₂: Enters through stomata.
  • Water: Absorbed by roots (with N, P, etc.).

Main Events

  1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll.
  2. Conversion of light to chemical energy + Splitting of water (H₂O → H₂ + O₂).
  3. Reduction of CO₂ to Carbohydrates.

Stomata

Tiny pores on the leaf surface surrounded by Guard Cells.

Functions

  • Gas Exchange: Massive exchange of gases (O₂/CO₂).
  • Transpiration: Loss of large amounts of water vapor.

Types of Heterotrophic Nutrition

Holozoic

Organisms take in solid food and break it down inside the body.
Ex: Amoeba, Animals, Humans.

Saprophytic

Feed on dead and decaying matter. Break down food outside the body.
Ex: Fungi (Bread moulds, Yeast).

Parasitic

Live inside/outside host and derive nutrition without killing it.
Ex: Cuscuta (Amarbel), Ticks, Leeches.

Nutrition in Amoeba

Unicellular organism using Holozoic nutrition.

  1. Ingestion: Takes in food using temporary finger-like extensions (Pseudopodia).
  2. Digestion: Food vacuole forms; complex substances broken down.
  3. Absorption: Diffuse into cytoplasm.
  4. Assimilation: Used for energy/growth.
  5. Egestion: Undigested material thrown out.

5.2 Respiration

Respiration

The process of releasing energy from food.

What is Respiration?

  • (i) Gaseous Exchange: Intake of oxygen from the atmosphere and release of CO₂ → Breathing.
  • (ii) Cellular Respiration: Breakdown of simple food in order to release energy inside the cell.

Aerobic vs Anaerobic Respiration

Aerobic Anaerobic
Takes place in the presence of oxygen. Takes place in the absence of oxygen.
Occurs in mitochondria. Occurs in cytoplasm.
End products are CO₂ and H₂O. End products are alcohol or lactic acid.
More amount of energy is released. Less amount of energy is released.

Mechanism of Breathing

Inhalation Exhalation
During inhalation the thoracic cavity (chest cavity) expands. Thoracic cavity contracts.
Ribs lift up. Ribs move downwards.
Diaphragm becomes flat in shape. Diaphragm becomes dome shaped.
Volume of lungs increases and air enters the lungs. Volume of lungs decreases and air exits from the lungs.

Exchange of Gases

Between alveolus, blood and tissues.

(i) Oxygen Transport

Air rich in O₂ (in alveolus) → Enters in blood (through blood vessels) → O₂ Binds with haemoglobin in RBC → O₂ released in tissues → Glucose Breakdown → CO₂ Production (in tissues)

(ii) CO₂ Transport

CO₂ from tissues released in blood and gets dissolved → Carried through blood vessels → Released in Alveolar sac → Sent out through nostrils

Respiration in Other Organisms

  • Terrestrial Organisms: Use atmospheric oxygen for respiration.
  • Aquatic Organisms: Use dissolved oxygen for respiration.

Respiration in Plants

Simpler than in animals. Gaseous exchange occurs through:

  1. Stomata in leaves.
  2. Lenticels in stems.
  3. General surface of the root.
5.3 Transportation

Transportation

Transport of food, oxygen, and waste.

Human Circulatory System

Consists of:

Heart (Pumping organ)
Arteries & Veins (Blood vessels)
Blood & Lymph (Circulatory medium)

Double Circulation

Blood travels twice through the heart in one complete cycle of the body.

  • Pulmonary Circulation: Blood moves from the heart to the lungs and back to the heart.
  • Systemic Circulation: Blood moves from the heart to the rest of the body and back to the heart.

Blood

A fluid connective tissue.

Solid Component (Blood Corpuscles)

RBCs

Carries gas (O₂, CO₂). Contains Hb (red colour).

Blood Platelets

Helps in Blood Clotting.

WBC

Provide body defence by engulfing germs & producing antibodies.

Liquid Component (Plasma)

Plasma

A yellow colour fluid containing 90% water & 10% organic substances like plasma proteins (albumin, globulin), inorganic mineral ions.

Lymph

A yellowish fluid escapes from the blood capillaries into the intercellular spaces, contains less proteins than blood. Lymph flows from the tissues to the heart, assisting in transportation and destroying germs.

Blood Vessels: Arteries vs Veins

Arteries Veins
1. Carry oxygenated blood from heart to body parts (except pulmonary artery).
2. Also called distributing vessel.		 1. Carry deoxygenated blood from body parts to heart (except pulmonary vein).
2. Also called collecting vessel.
3. Thick and elastic. 3. Thin and less elastic.
4. Deep seated. 4. Superficial as compared to arteries.

Transportation in Plants

There are two main conducting pathways in a plant.

Xylem Phloem
1. Carries water & minerals from the roots to other parts of the plant. 1. Carries product of photosynthesis from leaves to the other parts of the plant.
2. No energy is used. 2. Energy is used from ATP.
Transpiration

The process of loss of water as vapour from aerial parts of the plant.

  • Absorption and upward movement of water/minerals (osmotic pressure pull).
  • Helps in temperature regulation.
Translocation

Transport of food from leaves (food factory) to different part of the plant.

5.4 Excretion

Excretion

Removal of harmful metabolic wastes from the body.

What is Excretion?

The biological process involved in the removal of harmful metabolic wastes from the body is called excretion.

Material Excreted

Nitrogenous Wastes
Urea Uric Acid Urine
Excess Water
Sweat Urine
Gases CO₂
Salts Excess Salts

Excretory System in Humans

Consists of:

  • (1) The Kidneys: The excretory organ.
  • (2) The Ureters: Ducts which drain out urine from kidneys.
  • (3) The Urinary Bladder: The urine reservoir.
  • (4) The Urethra: The channel to the exterior.

Functions:

  1. Maintains water, pH, ionic, and osmotic equilibrium of blood.
  2. Excretes waste product urea in dissolved form.
  3. Excretes poisonous substances like drugs, toxins.
  4. Regulates blood pressure by controlling fluid balance.

Urine Formation (Nephron)

Each kidney contains many filtration units called Nephrons.

Structure of Nephron

  • Glomerulus: Cluster of thin-walled capillaries.
  • Bowman's Capsule: Cup-like structure associated with Glomerulus.
  • Tubular Part: PCT, Loop of Henle, DCT.
  • Collecting Duct: Where urine is collected.

Mechanism

1. Glomerular Filtration

Nitrogenous wastes, glucose, water, amino acids filtered into Bowman's Capsule.

2. Tubular Reabsorption

Useful substances (glucose, amino acids, salts, water) reabsorbed by capillaries.

3. Secretion

Urea, extra water, and salts secreted into tubule → Collecting Duct → Ureter.

Artificial Kidney (Haemodialysis) The process of purifying blood by an artificial kidney. It is meant for kidney failure patients.

Excretion in Plants

Plants use different strategies for excretion of different products:

  • Gaseous Wastes: Oxygen and CO₂ diffused through stomata.
  • Excess Water: Removed by transpiration.
  • Old Parts: Plants lose old leaves and bark.
  • Stored Wastes: Resins and gums (especially in old xylem).
  • Soil Excretion: Some waste substances excreted into the soil around them.
