5.1 Nutrition

Nutrition

The process by which an organism obtains its food.

Modes of Nutrition

1. Autotrophic

Organisms synthesize their own food from inorganic materials (CO₂, H₂O) using sunlight.
Ex: Green Plants.

2. Heterotrophic

Organisms depend on others for food. Cannot synthesize their own.
Ex: Animals, Fungi.

Autotrophic Nutrition (Photosynthesis)

Process of converting simple inorganic material into complex high-energy molecules (Carbohydrates).

6CO₂ + 12H₂O Sunlight/Chlorophyll C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ + 6H₂O

Raw Materials

  • Sunlight: Source of energy.
  • Chlorophyll: Absorbs light energy.
  • CO₂: Enters through stomata.
  • Water: Absorbed by roots (with N, P, etc.).

Main Events

  1. Absorption of light energy by chlorophyll.
  2. Conversion of light to chemical energy + Splitting of water (H₂O → H₂ + O₂).
  3. Reduction of CO₂ to Carbohydrates.

Stomata

Tiny pores on the leaf surface surrounded by Guard Cells.

Functions

  • Gas Exchange: Massive exchange of gases (O₂/CO₂).
  • Transpiration: Loss of large amounts of water vapor.

Types of Heterotrophic Nutrition

Holozoic

Organisms take in solid food and break it down inside the body.
Ex: Amoeba, Animals, Humans.

Saprophytic

Feed on dead and decaying matter. Break down food outside the body.
Ex: Fungi (Bread moulds, Yeast).

Parasitic

Live inside/outside host and derive nutrition without killing it.
Ex: Cuscuta (Amarbel), Ticks, Leeches.

Nutrition in Amoeba

Unicellular organism using Holozoic nutrition.

  1. Ingestion: Takes in food using temporary finger-like extensions (Pseudopodia).
  2. Digestion: Food vacuole forms; complex substances broken down.
  3. Absorption: Diffuse into cytoplasm.
  4. Assimilation: Used for energy/growth.
  5. Egestion: Undigested material thrown out.

5.2 Respiration

Respiration

Release of energy from food.

Types of Respiration

  • Aerobic: In presence of O₂. Produces CO₂, H₂O, High Energy (Mitochondria).
  • Anaerobic: In absence of O₂. Produces Ethanol/Lactic Acid, Low Energy (Yeast/Muscle cells).
Why do muscles cramp? Lack of oxygen during heavy exercise leads to anaerobic respiration in muscles, producing Lactic Acid.

Human Respiratory System

Nostrils → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Lungs → Alveoli.

Alveoli Balloon-like structures where exchange of gases (O₂ and CO₂) takes place. Large surface area.
5.3 Transportation

Transportation in Humans & Plants

Human Heart

Pumping organ. Double circulation.

  • Right Side: Deoxygenated blood (Vena Cava → RA → RV → Pulmonary Artery).
  • Left Side: Oxygenated blood (Pulmonary Vein → LA → LV → Aorta).

Transport in Plants

  • Xylem: Transports water & minerals (Unidirectional). Driven by Transpiration pull.
  • Phloem: Transports food (Bidirectional). Uses energy (ATP). Process called Translocation.
5.4 Excretion

Excretion

Removal of toxic wastes.

Human Excretory System

Kidneys filter blood to produce urine.

  • Nephron: Filtration unit. Glomerulus filters blood, tubular part reabsorbs glucose, amino acids, water.
  • Urine: Ureters → Bladder → Urethra.

Excretion in Plants

  • O₂ released via stomata.
  • Excess water via transpiration.
  • Wastes stored in old leaves (fall off) or vacuoles.
  • Resins and gums in old xylem.
