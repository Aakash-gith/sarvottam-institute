Control and Coordination

Explore how living organisms control and coordinate their body movements and metabolism through nervous and hormonal systems.

Start Learning View Activities

At a Glance

  • Nervous System: Fast control via electrical impulses.
  • Reflex Action: Rapid, involuntary response.
  • Hormones: Chemical coordination in plants & animals.
  • Tropisms: Plant movements linked to growth.
6.1 Animals – Nervous System

Nervous System

The control center that receives information from the environment and directs the body's response.

The Basics

Animals use Nervous Tissue and Muscular Tissue for control and coordination.

SENSE ORGANS & RECEPTORS
  • Gustatory: Taste (Tongue)
  • Olfactory: Smell (Nose)
  • Photoreceptors: Light (Eyes)
  • Phonoreceptors: Sound (Ears)
  • Thermoreceptors: Heat/Cold (Skin)

Neuron (Nerve Cell)

The structural and functional unit of the nervous system.

  • Dendrites: Acquire information.
  • Cell Body: Processes information.
  • Axon: Travels primarily as electrical impulse.
  • Synapse: Gap where signal converts to chemical (neurotransmitter) to cross to next neuron.

Reflex Actions

Sudden, involuntary action in response to a stimulus. Does not involve thinking (conscious) part of the brain initially.

REFLEX ARC PATHWAY

Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory Neuron → Spinal Cord → Motor Neuron → Effector (Muscle) → Response

The Human Brain

The main coordinating center of the body.

Part Region Function
Fore-brain Cerebrum Thinking, memory, voluntary actions, sensory interpretation, hunger centre.
Mid-brain - Relay center, auditory/visual reflexes.
Hind-brain Cerebellum Posture, balance, precision of voluntary movements (e.g., walking, riding).
Medulla Involuntary actions (blood pressure, salivation, vomiting).
Pons Regulation of respiration.

Protection of Tissues

  • Brain: Inside bony box (Cranium). Surrounded by fluid-filled balloon (shock absorber).
  • Spinal Cord: Protected by Vertebral Column (Backbone).

How Muscle Causes Action

  • Nerve impulse reaches muscle.
  • Muscle cells move by changing shape.
  • Mechanism: Special proteins change shape/arrangement in response to electrical impulses.
6.2 Coordination in Plants

Coordination in Plants

Plants lack a nervous system but use chemical means and movement for coordination.

Types of Plant Movements

1. Growth-Independent (Nastic)

Immediate response to stimulus. Not directional.
Ex: Touching sensitive plant (Mimosa).

2. Growth-Dependent (Tropic)

Directional movement due to growth.
Ex: Bending towards light.

Immediate Response

Sensitive Plant (Mimosa pudica): Leaves fold up and droop on touch.

  • No nervous tissue.
  • Uses electrical-chemical signals to convey info.
  • Mechanism: Cells change shape by changing water content (Turgor changes).

Tropic Movements

Directional growth in response to stimuli.

  • Phototropism: Response to Light (Shoots +, Roots -).
  • Geotropism: Response to Gravity (Roots +, Shoots -).
  • Hydrotropism: Response to Water (Roots +).
  • Chemotropism: Response to Chemicals (e.g., Pollen tube → Ovule).

Plant Hormones (Phytohormones)

Hormone Function
Auxins Synthesized at shoot tip. Helps cells grow longer. Causes bending towards light (Phototropism).
Gibberellins Growth of stem.
Cytokinins Promotes cell division (Rapid in fruits/seeds).
Abscisic Acid Inhibits growth. Causes wilting of leaves. (Stress hormone).
6.3 Hormones in Animals

Hormones in Animals

Endocrine glands secrete chemicals (hormones) directly into the blood.

Endocrine Glands

Major Hormones & Functions

Gland Hormone Function
Thyroid Thyroxin Regulates carbohydrate, protein, fat metabolism. (Need Iodine).
Pituitary Growth Hormone Regulates growth and development.
Adrenal Adrenaline "Fight or Flight". Increases heart rate, breathing, blood to muscles.
Pancreas Insulin Regulates blood sugar levels. Deficiency causing Diabetes.
Testes (Male) Testosterone Changes during puberty (Secondary sexual characters).
Ovaries (Female) Oestrogen Changes during puberty, Menstrual cycle.

Feedback Mechanism

Hormones must be secreted in precise quantities.

Mechanism: The timing and amount of hormone released are regulated by feedback mechanisms.

EXAMPLE: BLOOD SUGAR
  • Sugar level rises → Detected by Pancreas → More Insulin secreted.
  • Sugar level falls → Detected by Pancreas → Less Insulin secreted.
Recap

What You Have Learnt

  • Control and coordination are the functions of the nervous system and hormones.
  • The nervous system uses electrical impulses for rapid responses.
  • Reflexes are involuntary actions allowing quick protection.
  • The brain (CNS) integrates inputs and sends outputs.
  • Plants show tropisms (growth) and nastic (non-growth) movements.
  • Hormones provide chemical coordination in both plants and animals.

Exercises

Q1. Spinal Cord Injury

What happens if the spinal cord is injured?
Answer: Signals between the brain and body are disrupted, and reflex actions may be lost.

Q2. Phototropism

How does phototropism occur?
Answer: Auxin diffuses to the shady side, causing elongation there, bending the shoot towards light.

Q3. Chemical Coordination

Why is chemical coordination needed?
Answer: Electrical impulses cannot reach every cell; chemical signals diffuse to all cells for steady control.

Q4. Iodine Importance

Why is iodised salt advisable?
Answer: Iodine is needed for Thyroxin synthesis. Deficiency causes Goitre.
