Chapter 6 - Control and Coordination

Introduction

Control and coordination are essential for maintaining homeostasis and responding to environmental stimuli. Organisms use two main systems: nervous system and endocrine system.

Nervous System

The nervous system is a network of nerve cells (neurons) that transmit signals and coordinate all body activities.

Types of Nervous System

Central Nervous System (CNS) Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
Brain and spinal cord Nerves connecting CNS to body parts
Processes information Transmits signals to/from CNS
Integrating center Sensory and motor nerves

Brain Structure and Functions

  • Cerebrum: Controls voluntary actions, intelligence, memory, consciousness
    • Cerebral cortex: Grey matter (decision making)
    • White matter: Myelinated nerve fibers (transmission)
  • Cerebellum: Coordinates body movements, maintains balance and posture
  • Medulla Oblongata: Controls involuntary functions (breathing, heartbeat, digestion)
  • Thalamus: Relay station for sensory information
  • Hypothalamus: Temperature regulation, hormone control, emotional responses
Brain Protection: Protected by skull bones and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) which provides shock absorption and nutrients.

Spinal Cord

  • Structure: Cylindrical cord extending from brain to lower back
  • Function: Transmits signals between brain and body; coordinates reflexes
  • Grey Matter: Inner region (cell bodies, synapses)
  • White Matter: Outer region (myelinated axons)
  • Protection: Vertebral column (backbone)

Neurons

Neurons are basic units of the nervous system that transmit electrical and chemical signals.

Structure of Neuron:
  • Cell Body: Contains nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes
  • Dendrites: Branch-like extensions that receive signals from other neurons
  • Axon: Long projection that transmits signals to other neurons or muscles
  • Axon terminals: End branches that form synapses
  • Myelin sheath: Insulating layer around axon (increases speed of transmission)

Types of Neurons

  • Sensory Neurons: Receive information from receptors, transmit to CNS
  • Motor Neurons: Carry signals from CNS to muscles and glands
  • Relay Neurons: Connect sensory and motor neurons in CNS

Synapse and Neurotransmitters

A synapse is the junction between two neurons or between a neuron and a muscle cell.

Process of Signal Transmission:
  • 1. Electrical signal reaches axon terminal
  • 2. Synaptic vesicles release neurotransmitters
  • 3. Neurotransmitters cross synaptic cleft
  • 4. Bind to receptors on receiving neuron/muscle
  • 5. Generate new electrical signal or muscle contraction

Reflex Arc

A reflex is an automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that doesn't require brain involvement.

Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream.

Major Endocrine Glands

Gland Hormone Function
Pituitary Growth hormone, FSH, LH Growth, reproduction, metabolism
Thyroid Thyroxine Metabolism, growth, development
Parathyroid Parathormone (PTH) Calcium and phosphate balance
Adrenal Adrenaline, cortisol Stress response, metabolism
Pancreas Insulin, glucagon Blood glucose regulation
Ovaries/Testes Estrogen, testosterone Sexual characteristics, reproduction

Hormones and Their Effects

Hormones are chemical messengers that regulate various body functions.

  • Insulin: Decreases blood glucose by promoting uptake by cells
  • Glucagon: Increases blood glucose by promoting glycogen breakdown
  • Adrenaline: Prepares body for fight-or-flight response
  • Thyroxine: Controls metabolic rate and heat production
  • Growth Hormone: Promotes growth and development

Nervous vs Endocrine System Comparison

Feature Nervous System Endocrine System
Transmission Electrical + chemical signals Chemical hormones
Speed Very fast (milliseconds) Slow (seconds to minutes)
Duration Short-lived Long-lasting
Area of effect Localized Widespread
Example Quick reflexes Puberty changes

Plant Hormones (Auxins)

Plants also have control systems using hormones.

  • Auxins: Promote cell elongation, responsible for phototropism (growth toward light)
  • Gibberellins: Promote germination and flowering
  • Cytokinins: Promote cell division
  • Ethylene: Controls fruit ripening
  • Abscisic acid: Stress response hormone
