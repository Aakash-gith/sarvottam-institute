Fundamentals

Introduction to Chemical Reactions

Understanding how substances transform into new materials.

What is a Chemical Reaction?

A process in which one or more substances (reactants) are converted into new substances (products) with new chemical properties.

  • Atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed (Law of Conservation of Mass).
  • Occurs to achieve stable electronic configuration.

Chemical Changes in Daily Life

Chemical reactions happen around us all the time:

  • Souring of Milk: When left at room temperature in summer.
  • Rusting of Iron: Tawa/pan/nail left exposed to humid atmosphere.
  • Fermentation: Grapes getting fermented.
  • Cooking: Food is cooked.
  • Digestion: Food gets digested in our body.
  • Respiration: We respire.

Key Features

A chemical reaction is characterized by:

  • Change in State: Solid → Liquid → Gas.
  • Change in Colour: New substance has different colour.
  • Evolution of Gas: Bubbles or fumes.
  • Change in Temperature: Heat released or absorbed.
Exam Reminder If new substances are formed, it is a chemical change, not just physical.
Observations

Indicators of a Chemical Reaction

How do we know a reaction has taken place?

1. Change in Colour

Example: Brown Fe3+ ions react with colourless OH- to form brown Fe(OH)3.

2. Formation of Precipitate

Example: AgNO3 + NaCl → AgCl(s) + NaNO3 (White ppt).

3. Evolution of Gas

Example: Zn + H2SO4 → ZnSO4 + H2↑

4. Change in Temperature

  • Exothermic: Heat released (e.g., combustion).
  • Endothermic: Heat absorbed (e.g., decomposition).

5. Change in State

Solid → Liquid → Gas transformations during chemical changes.

Representation

Chemical Equations & Balancing

The language of chemistry.

Types of Equations

  • Word Equation: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium Oxide
  • Skeletal Equation: Mg + O2 → MgO (Unbalanced)
  • Balanced Equation: 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO
Why Balance? To satisfy the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Balancing Chemical Equations (NCERT Method)

Follow these steps to balance any equation:

  1. Step I: Draw boxes around each formula. Do not change anything inside the boxes.
  2. Step II: List the number of atoms of different elements.
  3. Step III: Start balancing with the compound having the maximum number of atoms.
  4. Step IV: Balance the other elements one by one.
  5. Step V: Check the correctness by counting atoms on both sides.
  6. Step VI: Write symbols of physical states (s, l, g, aq).
  7. Step VII: Write reaction conditions (temp, pressure, catalyst) above/below the arrow.
Example: Steam on Iron 3Fe(s) + 4H2O(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H2(g)
Classification

Types of Chemical Reactions

Classifying reactions based on how atoms are rearranged.

1. Combination Reaction

Two or more reactants combine to form a single product.

A + B → AB

Examples
  • Burning of Coal: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)
  • Formation of Water: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)
  • Slaked Lime: CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq)

Exothermic Reactions

Reactions in which heat is released along with the formation of products.

  • Burning of Natural Gas: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O
  • Respiration: Glucose breaks down to release energy.
    C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy

2. Decomposition Reaction

A single reactant breaks down to give simpler products.

AB → A + B

Endothermic These reactions require energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity to break bonds.

Types of Decomposition

  • Thermal (Heat): FeSO4, CaCO3, Pb(NO3)2.
  • Electrolytic (Electricity): Electrolysis of Water (2H2O → 2H2 + O2).
  • Photolytic (Light): AgCl, AgBr (Used in B&W photography).
    2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl2

3. Displacement Reaction

A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

A + BC → AC + B

Examples

  • Iron + Copper Sulphate:
    Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Zinc + Copper Sulphate:
    Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Lead + Copper Chloride:
    Pb(s) + CuCl2(aq) → PbCl2(aq) + Cu(s)

Zn and Pb are more reactive than Cu.

4. Double Displacement Reaction

Exchange of ions between reactants.

AB + CD → AD + CB

Precipitation Reaction

Any reaction that produces a precipitate (insoluble solid).

Example Na2SO4 + BaCl2 → BaSO4(s) + 2NaCl
White ppt of Barium Sulphate is formed.

5. Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

Definitions

  • Oxidation: Gain of Oxygen OR Loss of Hydrogen.
  • Reduction: Loss of Oxygen OR Gain of Hydrogen.

Examples

  • Copper Oxide + Hydrogen:
    CuO + H2 → Cu + H2O
    (CuO reduced to Cu, H2 oxidized to H2O)
  • Zinc Oxide + Carbon:
    ZnO + C → Zn + CO
  • Manganese Dioxide + HCl:
    MnO2 + 4HCl → MnCl2 + 2H2O + Cl2

Applications

Oxidation in Everyday Life

1. Corrosion

Gradual deterioration of metals due to reaction with air, moisture, or chemicals.

Examples: Rusting of Iron (Reddish-brown), Black coating on Silver, Green coating on Copper.

Prevention: Painting, Oiling, Greasing, Galvanization, Chrome plating, Anodizing, Alloying.

2. Rancidity

Oxidation of fats and oils in food causing unpleasant smell and taste.

Prevention Methods:

  • Adding antioxidants (BHA, BHT).
  • Storing food in air-tight containers.
  • Flushing bags of chips with Nitrogen gas.
  • Keeping food away from direct sunlight.

Practice Questions

Click to reveal answers.
1. Why is respiration considered an exothermic reaction?
+
Because energy is released during the breakdown of glucose in our cells. C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + Energy
2. Why do we apply paint on iron articles?
+
To prevent rusting. Paint cuts off the contact of iron with air and moisture.
3. Oil and fat containing food items are flushed with Nitrogen. Why?
+
To prevent rancidity. Nitrogen is an inert gas and prevents oxidation of fats and oils.
4. What is the colour of Copper Sulphate solution?
+
Blue. When iron is added, it turns pale green due to formation of Ferrous Sulphate.
