Fundamentals

Acids and Bases

Chemical substances with distinct properties.

Acids

Substances that produce H+ ions (or Hydronium ions H3O+) in aqueous solution.

  • Taste: Sour (Latin: acidus).
  • Litmus: Turns Blue Litmus Red.
  • Examples: HCl, H2SO4, Lemon Juice (Citric Acid).

Bases

Substances that produce OH- ions in aqueous solution.

  • Taste: Bitter & Soapy to touch.
  • Litmus: Turns Red Litmus Blue.
  • Examples: NaOH, Ca(OH)2, Soap.
  • Alkalis: Bases soluble in water (e.g., NaOH, KOH).

Classification

  • Strong Acids: HCl, H2SO4, HNO3
  • Weak Acids: CH3COOH, Oxalic acid, Lactic acid
  • Strong Bases: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)2
  • Weak Bases: NH4OH

Concentration

  • Concentrated: More acid + Less water.
  • Dilute: More water + Less acid.

Reactions

Chemical Properties

1. Reaction with Metals

Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas

Observation Hydrogen gas burns with a pop sound.
Zn + H2SO4 → ZnSO4 + H2

Base + Metal:

2NaOH + Zn → Na2ZnO2 + H2
(Sodium Zincate)

2. Reaction with Carbonates

Acid + Metal Carbonate → Salt + CO2 + Water

Observation CO2 turns lime water milky.
Na2CO3 + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2
Note Bases do not react with Metal Carbonates or Metal Hydrogen Carbonates.

3. Neutralization

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O
Nature of Salt:
  • Strong Acid + Weak Base → Acidic Salt (pH < 7)
  • Weak Acid + Strong Base → Basic Salt (pH > 7)
  • Strong Acid + Strong Base → Neutral Salt (pH = 7)

4. Reaction with Oxides

  • Metal Oxides (Basic): React with Acids.
    CuO + 2HCl → CuCl2 + H2O
  • Non-metal Oxides (Acidic): React with Bases.
    CO2 + Ca(OH)2 → CaCO3 + H2O
Strength

The pH Scale

Measuring H+ ion concentration.
Acidic
pH < 7
Neutral
pH = 7
Basic
pH > 7

Importance in Everyday Life

  • Stomach: HCl helps digestion. Excess causes acidity (Treat with Antacid like Mg(OH)2).
  • Tooth Decay: Starts when mouth pH < 5.5. Bacteria produce acid from sugar.
  • Soil: Plants need specific pH. Acidic soil is treated with Quick Lime.
  • Self Defense: Ant sting / Nettle leaves inject Methanoic Acid. Treat with Baking Soda.
  • Acid Rain: When pH of rain water is less than 5.6, it is called acid rain.

Dilution of Acids

Mixing an acid with water decreases the concentration of ions (H3O+ / OH-) per unit volume.

⚠️ SAFETY WARNING Always add Acid to Water slowly with stirring.
Never add Water to Acid! The reaction is highly exothermic and may cause splashing or break the glass container.
Compounds

Common Salts

Chlor-Alkali Process

Electrolysis of aqueous NaCl (Brine) produces NaOH, Cl2, and H2.

2NaCl(aq) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl2(g) + H2(g)

Bleaching Powder

CaOCl2

Prep: Action of Chlorine on dry Slaked Lime.

Uses: Bleaching cotton/linen, Oxidizing agent, Disinfecting water.

Baking Soda

NaHCO3

Uses: Making baking powder (with tartaric acid), Antacid, Soda-acid fire extinguishers.

2NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + H2O + CO2 (On heating)

Washing Soda

Na2CO3.10H2O

Uses: Glass/Soap/Paper industry, Removing permanent hardness of water.

Na2CO3 + 10H2O → Na2CO3.10H2O (Recrystallisation)

Plaster of Paris

CaSO4.½H2O

Chemical Name: Calcium sulphate hemihydrate.

Prep: Heating Gypsum (CaSO4.2H2O) at 373K.

Property: White powder. Changes to Gypsum on mixing with water.

Uses: Supporting fractured bones, Making toys/decoration, Making surfaces smooth.

CaSO4.½H2O + 1½H2O → CaSO4.2H2O (Setting)

Water of Crystallization

Fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.

  • Blue Vitriol: CuSO4.5H2O (5 H2O).
  • Washing Soda: Na2CO3.10H2O (10 H2O).
  • Gypsum: CaSO4.2H2O (2 H2O).
