Activity 2.1

Testing with Indicators

Aim

To test various solutions with Blue/Red Litmus, Phenolphthalein, and Methyl Orange.

Procedure

Collect samples of HCl, H2SO4, HNO3, CH3COOH, NaOH, Ca(OH)2, KOH, Mg(OH)2, and NH4OH. Test each with the indicators.

Observation Table

Sample Red Litmus Blue Litmus Phenol. M. Orange
Acid No Change Red Colourless Red
Base Blue No Change Pink Yellow
Activity 2.2

Olfactory Indicators

Aim

To check if smell changes in acidic or basic medium using Onion, Vanilla, and Clove oil.

Observation:
  • Acidic Medium: Smell of onion/vanilla/clove remains.
  • Basic Medium: Smell vanishes (cannot be detected).

Conclusion: These substances can be used as olfactory indicators by visually impaired students.

Activity 2.3 & 2.4

Reaction with Metals

Procedure

Take dilute H2SO4 in a test tube and add Zinc granules. Pass the gas through soap solution.

Repeat with NaOH solution and Zinc granules (warm it).

Chemical Equations

Zn + H2SO4 → ZnSO4 + H2
Zn + 2NaOH → Na2ZnO2 + H2
Observation:

Bubbles are seen on zinc granules. Gas evolved burns with a pop sound.

Conclusion:

Metals react with acids and bases to produce Hydrogen gas.

Activity 2.5

Metal Carbonates with Acid

Procedure

Take Na2CO3 and NaHCO3 in separate test tubes. Add dilute HCl. Pass the gas through Lime Water.

Reactions

Na2CO3 + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H2O + CO2
Ca(OH)2 + CO2 → CaCO3(s) + H2O
Observation:

Brisk effervescence is observed. Lime water turns milky due to formation of white precipitate of CaCO3.

On passing excess CO2, milkiness disappears (forms soluble Ca(HCO3)2).

Activity 2.6

Neutralization Reaction

Procedure

Take NaOH solution (Pink with Phenolphthalein). Add dilute HCl drop by drop.

Observation:

The pink colour disappears when enough acid is added. The solution becomes colourless.

Adding NaOH again makes it pink.

Conclusion:

Acid neutralizes the effect of Base.

NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H2O
Activity 2.7

Metal Oxide with Acid

Procedure

Take Copper Oxide (Black) in a beaker. Add dilute HCl and stir.

Observation:

The black oxide dissolves and the solution turns blue-green.

Conclusion:

Metal oxides are basic in nature.

CuO + 2HCl → CuCl2 (Blue-Green) + H2O
Activity 2.8

Electrical Conductivity

Procedure

Connect electrodes to a bulb and battery. Dip in solutions of Glucose, Alcohol, HCl, and NaOH.

Observation:
  • Bulb Glows: HCl, NaOH (Conduct electricity).
  • Bulb does NOT Glow: Glucose, Alcohol (Do not conduct).
Conclusion:

Acids and Bases produce ions (H+ / OH-) in water which conduct electricity. Glucose/Alcohol do not ionize.

Activity 2.9

Preparation of HCl Gas

Procedure

React solid NaCl with Conc. H2SO4. Test evolved gas with dry and wet blue litmus paper.

2NaCl + H2SO4 → Na2SO4 + 2HCl(g)
Observation:
  • Dry Litmus: No colour change.
  • Wet Litmus: Turns Red.
Conclusion:

Acids show acidic properties only in the presence of water (formation of H3O+ ions).

Activity 2.10

Dilution of Acid

Procedure

Take water in a beaker. Add Conc. H2SO4 slowly with stirring. Touch the beaker.

Observation:

The beaker becomes hot.

Conclusion:

Dilution of acid is a highly exothermic process. Always add Acid to Water, never Water to Acid.

Activity 2.11 & 2.12

pH of Solutions and Soil

Procedure

Test pH of saliva, lemon juice, coffee, tomato juice, tap water, NaOH, and HCl using Universal Indicator.

Collect soil sample, dissolve in water, filter, and check pH.

Conclusion:

Plants require a specific pH range for healthy growth. If soil is acidic, treat with Quick Lime (CaO) or Slaked Lime.

Activity 2.13 & 2.14

Family and pH of Salts

Procedure

Check formula and pH of various salts: NaCl, KNO3, AlCl3, ZnSO4, CuSO4, CH3COONa, Na2CO3.

Observation:
  • Neutral Salts (pH 7): NaCl, KNO3 (Strong Acid + Strong Base).
  • Acidic Salts (pH < 7): NH4Cl, CuSO4 (Strong Acid + Weak Base).
  • Basic Salts (pH > 7): Na2CO3, CH3COONa (Weak Acid + Strong Base).
Activity 2.15

Water of Crystallization

Procedure

Heat blue crystals of Copper Sulphate in a dry test tube. Observe colour change and water droplets.

Add 2-3 drops of water to the white powder obtained.

(Diagram: Heating CuSO4 crystals)

Reaction

CuSO4.5H2O (Blue) → CuSO4 (White) + 5H2O
Observation:

Blue crystals turn white on heating. Water droplets appear on test tube walls.

On adding water, blue colour is restored.

Conclusion:

Salts contain fixed number of water molecules (Water of Crystallization).
