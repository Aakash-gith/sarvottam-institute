NCERT Activities

Detailed breakdown of Activity 3.1 to 3.14

Activity 3.1

Metallic Lustre

Physical Property
Procedure
  1. Take samples of Iron, Copper, Aluminium, and Magnesium.
  2. Note the appearance of each sample.
  3. Clean the surface of each sample by rubbing them with sand paper and note their appearance again.
Observation

The metals appear dull initially due to oxide layers. After rubbing with sandpaper, they become shiny and reflective.

Conclusion

Metals, in their pure state, have a shining surface. This property is called Metallic Lustre.

Activity 3.2

Malleability

Physical Property
Procedure
  1. Take small pieces of Iron, Copper, Aluminium, and Magnesium.
  2. Place the metal on a block of iron and strike it four or five times with a hammer.
  3. Observe the change in the shape of these metals.
Observation

The metals flatten into thin sheets without breaking.

Conclusion

Metals can be beaten into thin sheets. This property is called Malleability. Gold and Silver are the most malleable metals.

Activity 3.3 & 3.4

Hardness & Ductility

Physical Property
Procedure
  1. Hardness: Try to cut metals with a sharp knife. Try cutting Sodium with a knife.
  2. Ductility: Consider which metals are available in the form of wires.
Observation

Most metals are hard and cannot be cut (Fe, Cu). Sodium (Na) is soft and can be cut easily. Metals like Cu, Al, Ag can be drawn into wires.

Conclusion

Metals are generally hard (Exception: Alkali metals). Metals can be drawn into thin wires (Ductility). Gold is the most ductile metal.

Activity 3.5

Thermal Conductivity

Heat Transfer
Procedure
  1. Clamp a metal rod (Aluminium or Copper) on a stand.
  2. Fix a pin to the free end of the rod using wax.
  3. Heat the rod with a burner near the clamped end.
  4. Observe what happens to the wax and the pin.
Observation

The heat travels along the rod, melting the wax. The pin falls down. The metal rod does not melt.

Conclusion

Metals are good conductors of heat and have high melting points. Silver and Copper are the best conductors.

Activity 3.6

Electrical Conductivity

Electricity
Procedure
  1. Set up an electric circuit with a battery, bulb, and switch.
  2. Place the metal to be tested between the terminals (clips).
  3. Switch on the current and observe the bulb.
Observation

The bulb glows when metals are placed in the circuit.

Conclusion

Metals are good conductors of electricity.

Activity 3.8

Burning of Metals (Oxides)

Chemical Property
Procedure
  1. Hold a Magnesium ribbon with tongs and burn it.
  2. Collect the ash formed.
  3. Dissolve the ash in water and test with litmus paper.
Observation

Magnesium burns with a dazzling white flame. White powder (MgO) is formed. The solution turns Red Litmus Blue.

Conclusion

Metals react with oxygen to form Metal Oxides. Metal oxides are generally basic in nature.

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO
Activity 3.9

Reaction with Oxygen (Reactivity)

Reactivity Series
Procedure
  1. Heat samples of various metals (Al, Cu, Fe, Pb, Mg, Zn, Na) over a flame.
  2. Observe the reaction intensity and flame color.
Observation

Na: Burns vigorously (kept in kerosene).
Mg: Burns with white flame.
Al, Zn, Pb: Do not burn, form oxide layer.
Fe: Does not burn, filings burn vigorously.
Cu: Does not burn, coated with black oxide.

Conclusion

Metals show different reactivities towards oxygen. Order: Na > Mg > Zn > Fe > Cu.

Activity 3.10

Reaction with Water

Chemical Property
Procedure
  1. Put small pieces of metals in cold water.
  2. If no reaction, put them in hot water.
  3. If still no reaction, treat with steam (using the setup shown).
Observation

Na, K: React violently with cold water (Exothermic).
Ca: Reacts less violently, floats.
Mg: Reacts with hot water, floats.
Al, Fe, Zn: React only with steam.

Conclusion

Metals react with water to form Metal Oxide/Hydroxide and Hydrogen. Reactivity decreases from K to Fe.

Activity 3.11

Reaction with Acids

Chemical Property
Procedure
  1. Collect metals (Mg, Al, Zn, Fe, Cu).
  2. Add dilute Hydrochloric Acid (HCl) to test tubes containing metals.
  3. Observe the rate of bubble formation (Hydrogen gas).
Observation

Mg: Vigorous bubbling (Fastest).
Al, Zn, Fe: Rate decreases in this order.
Cu: No bubbles (No reaction).

Conclusion

Metals react with dilute acids to give Salt + Hydrogen. Cu is less reactive than Hydrogen.

Activity 3.12

Displacement Reaction

Reactivity Series
Procedure
  1. Take a clean Copper wire and an Iron nail.
  2. Put Copper wire in Iron Sulphate solution and Iron nail in Copper Sulphate solution.
  3. Observe after 20 minutes.
Observation

In the test tube with Iron nail + CuSO4, the blue color fades to green (FeSO4) and a brown coating (Cu) forms on the nail. No change in the other tube.

Conclusion

Iron is more reactive than Copper and displaces it from its salt solution.

Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
Activity 3.13

Properties of Ionic Compounds

Ionic Bonding
Procedure
  1. Take samples of NaCl, KCl, BaCl2.
  2. Heat a small amount on a spatula.
  3. Dissolve in water.
  4. Test electrical conductivity in solution.
Observation

They have high melting points (do not melt easily). They are soluble in water. Their solutions conduct electricity (bulb glows).

Conclusion

Ionic compounds are hard solids, have high MP/BP, are soluble in water, and conduct electricity in aqueous state.

Activity 3.14

Corrosion

Degradation
Procedure
  1. Take 3 test tubes with Iron nails.
  2. A: Water + Air.
  3. B: Boiled water + Oil (No Air).
  4. C: Anhydrous CaCl2 (Dry Air).
  5. Leave for a few days.
Observation

Nails rust only in test tube A. Nails in B and C do not rust.

Conclusion

Both Air (Oxygen) and Moisture (Water) are essential for rusting of iron.
