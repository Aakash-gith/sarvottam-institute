Characteristics

Physical Properties

Comparing Metals and Non-metals.

Metals

  • Lustre: Shiny surface (e.g., Gold, Silver).
  • Hardness: Generally hard (Exception: Na, K, Li are soft).
  • Malleability: Can be beaten into thin sheets (Au, Ag).
  • Ductility: Can be drawn into thin wires (Au is most ductile).
  • Conductivity: Good conductors of heat (Ag, Cu) and electricity.
  • Sonority: Produce sound on striking.

Non-metals

  • Lustre: Dull (Exception: Iodine, Diamond).
  • Hardness: Generally soft (Exception: Diamond is hardest natural substance).
  • State: Solids or gases (Exception: Bromine is liquid).
  • Conductivity: Poor conductors (Exception: Graphite conducts electricity).
Reactions

Chemical Properties of Metals

1. Reaction with Air

Metal + Oxygen → Metal Oxide

  • Cu: 2Cu + O2 → 2CuO (Black).
  • Al: 4Al + 3O2 → 2Al2O3.
Amphoteric Oxides React with both acids and bases (e.g., Al2O3, ZnO).

2. Reaction with Water

Metal + Water → Metal Oxide/Hydroxide + H2

  • Cold Water: Na, K (Violent), Ca (Floats).
  • Hot Water: Mg (Floats).
  • Steam: Al, Fe, Zn.
  • No Reaction: Pb, Cu, Ag, Au.

3. Reaction with Acids

Metal + Dilute Acid → Salt + H2

Why not HNO3? Nitric acid is a strong oxidizing agent. It oxidizes H2 to water. (Exception: Mg and Mn react with very dilute HNO3 to give H2).

4. Reactivity Series

Arrangement of metals in decreasing order of reactivity.

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > [H] > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au

Displacement: More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its salt solution.

Bonding

Ionic Compounds

Transfer of electrons from metal to non-metal.

Formation of Sodium Chloride (NaCl)

Na (2,8,1) loses 1e- → Na+ (2,8)
Cl (2,8,7) gains 1e- → Cl- (2,8,8)

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  • Physical Nature: Solid and hard due to strong inter-ionic attraction. Brittle.
  • Melting/Boiling Points: High. Considerable energy needed to break strong attraction.
  • Solubility: Soluble in water, insoluble in solvents like kerosene/petrol.
  • Conduction: Conduct electricity in molten or aqueous state (ions move), but not in solid state.
Metallurgy

Occurrence & Extraction

Enrichment of Ores

Removal of impurities (gangue) from the ore.

Methods Depends on physical/chemical properties of gangue and ore (e.g., Gravity separation, Froth floatation, Magnetic separation).

Extracting Metals

  • Low Reactivity: Heating alone (e.g., Cinnabar HgS → Hg).
  • Middle Reactivity:
    • Roasting: Sulphide ores heated in excess air (ZnS → ZnO).
    • Calcination: Carbonate ores heated in limited air (ZnCO3 → ZnO).
    • Reduction: Using Carbon (ZnO + C → Zn + CO).
  • High Reactivity: Electrolytic Reduction (e.g., NaCl → Na at cathode).

Thermite Reaction

Displacement reaction of Iron(III) oxide with Aluminium is highly exothermic. Used to join railway tracks.

Fe2O3(s) + 2Al(s) → 2Fe(l) + Al2O3(s) + Heat

Refining of Metals

Electrolytic Refining (e.g., Copper):

  • Anode: Impure metal.
  • Cathode: Strip of pure metal.
  • Electrolyte: Salt solution of the metal.
  • Anode Mud: Insoluble impurities settle at bottom.
Degradation

Corrosion & Prevention

Corrosion Examples

  • Silver: Black coating (Ag2S) reacting with Sulphur in air.
  • Copper: Green coating (CuCO3.Cu(OH)2) reacting with CO2.
  • Iron: Brown flaky substance (Rust) reacting with moist air.

Prevention

  • Painting, Oiling, Greasing.
  • Galvanization: Coating steel/iron with Zinc.
  • Alloying: Homogeneous mixture.
    • Brass: Cu + Zn
    • Bronze: Cu + Sn
    • Solder: Pb + Sn (Low melting point)
    • Stainless Steel: Fe + Ni + Cr
