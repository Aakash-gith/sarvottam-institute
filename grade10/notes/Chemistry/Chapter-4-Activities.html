Activity 4.1

Properties of Covalent Compounds

Physical Properties
üß™ Procedure
  1. Take samples of sugar, salt (NaCl), and naphthalene balls.
  2. Heat small quantities of each on separate spoons.
  3. Note the melting behavior and temperature.
  4. Test their solubility in water and kerosene separately.
Observation

Sugar and naphthalene melt easily at low temperature. Salt requires very high temperature. Sugar dissolves in water but not in kerosene. Naphthalene dissolves in kerosene but not in water.

Conclusion

Covalent compounds (sugar, naphthalene) have low melting points due to weak intermolecular forces. Ionic compounds (salt) have high melting points due to strong electrostatic attractions.

Solubility Rule: "Like dissolves like" - Polar compounds dissolve in polar solvents (water), non-polar in non-polar solvents (kerosene).

Activity 4.8

Reaction of Ethanoic Acid with Base

Acid-Base Reaction
‚öóÔ∏è Procedure
  1. Take 2 ml dilute ethanoic acid (acetic acid) in a test tube.
  2. Add a few drops of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solution drop by drop.

Test tube becomes warm to touch (exothermic reaction). No visible gas evolution. Solution becomes neutral when tested with litmus (turns neither red nor blue).

Ethanoic acid reacts with sodium hydroxide to form sodium acetate (salt) and water through neutralization reaction. Heat is released.

Activity 4.10

Cleaning Action of Soap - Micelle Formation

Practical Application
üßº Procedure
  1. Add 2-3 drops of cooking oil to water in a test tube.
  2. Shake vigorously and observe if oil mixes with water.
  3. Now add 5-6 drops of soap solution to the same tube.
  4. Shake again and observe the mixture.
ü´ß Micelle Structure
Observation

Without soap: Oil and water separate into two layers (oil floats on top).
With soap: Oil disperses into tiny droplets forming a cloudy, milky mixture (emulsion). Oil doesn't separate even after standing.

Conclusion

Soap molecules form spherical structures called micelles around oil droplets. Hydrophobic tails (non-polar) attach to oil inside, hydrophilic heads (polar) face water outside, making oil soluble in water and allowing it to be washed away.

Mechanism:
‚ë† Soap molecules surround oil droplets
‚ë° Form spherical micelles (tail inward, head outward)
‚ë¢ Oil trapped inside becomes water-soluble
‚ë£ Micelles wash away with water
