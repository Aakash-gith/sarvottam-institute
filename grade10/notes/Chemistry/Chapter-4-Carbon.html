4.1 Bonding in Carbon

The Covalent Bond

Why carbon forms covalent bonds instead of ionic bonds.

Why Covalent?

Allotropes of Carbon

Diamond

Each C bonded to 4 other C atoms. Rigid 3D structure. Hardest substance.

Graphite

Each C bonded to 3 other C atoms. Hexagonal arrays. Smooth, slippery, conducts electricity.

Fullerenes

C-60 (Buckminsterfullerene). Football shape.

4.2 Versatile Nature

Versatile Nature of Carbon

Catenation and Tetravalency lead to millions of compounds.

Key Properties

  • Catenation: Unique ability to form bonds with other carbon atoms (chains, branches, rings). Silicon shows this property but only up to 7-8 atoms and is reactive. Carbon bonds are very strong and stable.
  • Tetravalency: Valency of 4. Can bond with O, H, N, S, Cl, etc.

Saturated vs Unsaturated

  • Saturated: Single bonds only (Alkanes). Less reactive.
  • Unsaturated: Double or triple bonds (Alkenes, Alkynes). More reactive.

Detailed Nomenclature (IUPAC Rules)

The Golden Formula

Prefix + Word Root + Primary Suffix + Secondary Suffix

  • Prefix: Substituents (e.g., Chloro-, Methyl-)
  • Word Root: No. of Carbons (Meth-, Eth-, Prop-)
  • Pri. Suffix: Saturation (ane, ene, yne)
  • Sec. Suffix: Functional Group (-ol, -al, -oic acid)

Key Rules

  1. Longest Chain: Select the longest continuous carbon chain containing the functional group.
  2. Lowest Number: Number from the end giving the substituent/functional group the lowest number.
  3. Alphabetical Order: If multiple substituents, list them alphabetically (e.g., Bromo before Chloro).
  4. Priority Order: Carboxylic Acid > Aldehyde > Ketone > Alcohol > Alkene/Alkyne.

4.5 Soaps and Detergents

Cleansing Action

Soap Molecules

Sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic acids.

  • Ionic End: Hydrophilic (water-loving).
  • Carbon Chain: Hydrophobic (oil-loving).

Micelle Formation

Ionic ends face water, carbon chains dissolve in oil/dirt. Forms a structure called Micelle. Emulsion is formed, and dirt is washed away.
