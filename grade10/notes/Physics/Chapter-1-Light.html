Reflection of Light

Reflection is the phenomenon in which light bounces off a surface. The law of reflection governs how light behaves when it strikes a surface.

Laws of Reflection

First Law: The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal to the surface all lie in the same plane.

Second Law: Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection

Normal: A line perpendicular to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence

Types of Reflection

  • Regular (Specular) Reflection: Occurs on smooth, polished surfaces
    • Parallel rays remain parallel after reflection
    • Sharp, clear image formed
    • Example: Mirror, calm water surface
  • Diffuse (Irregular) Reflection: Occurs on rough, uneven surfaces
    • Parallel rays are reflected in different directions
    • No clear image formed
    • Example: Wall, paper, ground

Spherical Mirrors

Mirrors that have curved surfaces are called spherical mirrors. They are parts of a sphere with one polished surface.

  • Concave Mirror (Converging Mirror): Reflects light towards a focus
    • Center of curvature (C): Center of sphere
    • Focal length (f) = R/2 (where R = radius)
    • Forms real or virtual images
    • Used in: Torches, telescopes, shaving mirrors
  • Convex Mirror (Diverging Mirror): Reflects light away from mirror
    • Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image
    • Used in: Car rear-view mirrors, street lights

Terms Related to Spherical Mirrors

  • Pole (P): Center of reflecting surface
  • Center of Curvature (C): Center of sphere
  • Principal Axis: Line joining pole and center of curvature
  • Focal Point (F): Point where parallel rays converge/diverge
  • Focal Length (f): Distance from pole to focal point
  • Radius of Curvature (R): Distance from pole to center of curvature
  • Aperture: Size of the mirror

Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one transparent medium to another with different optical densities.

Causes of Refraction

Light travels at different speeds in different media. When light enters a new medium, its speed changes, causing the direction to change (unless perpendicular to surface).

Lens

A lens is a transparent medium bounded by two curved surfaces (or one curved and one plane). Used to converge or diverge light rays.

  • Convex (Converging) Lens: Bulges outward (thicker at center)
    • Converges parallel rays to a point (focal point)
    • Forms real or virtual images
    • Used in: Magnifying glass, cameras, projectors
  • Concave (Diverging) Lens: Curves inward (thinner at center)
    • Diverges parallel rays away
    • Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image
    • Used in: Glasses for myopia, peepholes
