11.1 Fundamentals

Electric Current and Circuit

Electric Current (I)

The rate of flow of electric charge is called electric current.

Formula I = Q / t
  • Q: Net charge (Coulombs, C)
  • t: Time (Seconds, s)
  • SI Unit: Ampere (A)

Direction: Conventional current flows from Positive terminal to Negative terminal (Opposite to electron flow).

Measurement & Circuit

  • Ammeter: Device used to measure current.
  • Connected in SERIES in a circuit.
  • It has very low resistance.
[Simple Electric Circuit]
11.2 Potential

Electric Potential Difference

Concept & Definition

Electric potential difference between two points in an electric circuit carrying some current is the work done to move a unit charge from one point to the other.

Analogy: Water flows from high level to low level; similarly, charge flows from high potential to low potential.

Formula V = W / Q
  • W: Work done (Joule, J)
  • Q: Charge (Coulomb, C)
  • SI Unit: Volt (V)

Voltmeter

  • Instrument used to measure potential difference.
  • Connected in PARALLEL across the points.
  • It has high resistance.
11.3 Components

Circuit Diagram

Standard symbols used to represent electrical components in circuit diagrams:

Key Components

  • Electric Cell/Battery: Source of potential difference.
  • Switch/Key: Makes or breaks the circuit.
  • Resistor: Opposes flow of current.
  • Rheostat: Variable resistance to control current.

Meters

  • Ammeter (A): Measures Current.
  • Voltmeter (V): Measures Voltage.
  • Galvanometer (G): Detects presence of current.
11.4 Relationship

Ohm's Law

Statement

The potential difference (V) across the ends of a given metallic wire in an electric circuit is directly proportional to the current flowing through it, provided its temperature remains the same.

V ∝ I
V = IR

Where R is the Resistance (constant for a given wire at a given temp).

V-I Graph

Straight line graph passing through origin indicates V ∝ I.

Resistance (R)

It is the property of a conductor to resist the flow of charges through it.

  • Examples: Variable Resistance (Rheostat) is used to change current without changing voltage source.
  • Good Conductor: Low Resistance.
  • Insulator: Very High Resistance.
SI Unit: Ohm (Ω) 1 Ω = 1 V / 1 A
11.5 Factors

Factors Affecting Resistance

Factors

The resistance of a conductor depends on:

  1. Length (l): Directly proportional (R ∝ l).
  2. Area of Cross-section (A): Inversely proportional (R ∝ 1/A).
  3. Nature of Material: Depends on atomic structure.
  4. Temperature: Resistance increases with temperature for metals.

Resistivity (ρ)

R = ρ (l / A)
  • ρ (Rho): Constant of proportionality called Electrical Resistivity.
  • SI Unit: Ohm-meter (Ω m).
  • Characteristic property of material.
  • Metals/Alloys: Low resistivity (10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ Ω m).
  • Insulators: High resistivity (10¹² to 10¹⁷ Ω m).
11.6 Combinations

Resistance of a System of Resistors

11.6.1 Resistors in Series

Resistors are joined end to end.

  • Current: Same in every part of the circuit.
  • Potential Difference: Sum of PD across individual resistors equals total voltage. (V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃)
Equivalent Resistance Rs = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Rs > Maximum individual resistance.

11.6.2 Resistors in Parallel

Resistors are connected between two common points.

  • Potential Difference: Same across each resistor.
  • Current: Sum of currents through branches equals total current. (I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃)
Equivalent Resistance 1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

Rp < Minimum individual resistance.

Advantages of Parallel Combination in Domestic Circuits:
  • Each appliance has its own switch ( Independent operation).
  • If one appliance fails, others continue to work.
  • Each appliance gets the same voltage (220V).
  • Total resistance is reduced, allowing required current for appliances.
11.7 Heating

Heating Effect of Electric Current

When electric current passes through a high resistance wire (like Nichrome), it becomes very hot and produces heat. This is the heating effect.

Joule's Law of Heating H = I²Rt

Heat produced (H) is directly proportional to:

  • Square of Current (I²)
  • Resistance (R)
  • Time (t) for which current flows

Other forms: H = VIt, H = (V²/R)t

Practical Applications

  • Electric Iron, Toaster, Heater: Use alloys (like Nichrome) which have high resistivity and don't oxidize at high temps.
  • Electric Bulb: Filament (Tungsten) retains heat to emit light. High melting point (3380°C).

Electric Fuse

Safety device connected in series.

  • Protects circuits from overloading and short-circuits.
  • Made of Lead-Tin alloy (low melting point).
  • Melts and breaks circuit if current exceeds rating.
11.8 Power

Electric Power

Definition & Formula

Rate at which electrical energy is consumed or dissipated.

P = VI
P = I²R = V²/R
  • SI Unit: Watt (W).
  • 1 W = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere (1V × 1A).

Commercial Unit of Energy

Kilowatt-hour (kWh) or 'Unit'.

1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J

Used for electricity bills.
