11.1 Fundamentals

Electric Charge and Current

Electric Charge (Q)

Charge is a fundamental particle in an atom. It may be positive or negative.

  • Like charges repel, unlike charges attract.
  • SI Unit: Coulomb (C).
  • Charge on 1 electron (e) = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
Quantization of Charge Q = ne
n = number of electrons
e = charge on 1 electron

Electric Current (I)

The rate of flow of charge is called current.

Formula I = Q / t
1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb / 1 second (1 Cs⁻¹)
  • Small Units:
    • 1 mA (Milliampere) = 10⁻³ A
    • 1 μA (Microampere) = 10⁻⁶ A
  • Direction: Opposite to flow of electrons. (Positive → Negative).

11.2 Potential

Potential Difference (V)

Definition

Work done to move a unit charge from one point to another.

V = W / Q

1 Volt Definition:
When 1 Joule of work is done in carrying 1 Coulomb charge, the potential difference is called 1 Volt.

Measurement

  • Voltmeter: Instrument to measure PD.
  • Connection: Always in PARALLEL.
  • Has high resistance.
  • Cells maintain potential difference in a circuit.
  • Current flows from Higher Potential → Lower Potential.

11.4 Relationship

Ohm's Law & Resistance

Statement

Potential difference across the two points of a metallic conductor is directly proportional to current passing through it, provided temperature remains constant.

V ∝ I
V = IR
[Diagram: V vs I Graph - Straight Line]

Resistance (R)

Property to resist flow of charges.

  • 1 Ohm (Ω): If potential difference is 1 V and current is 1 A, then resistance is 1 Ω.
  • Rheostat: Component to regulate current without changing voltage source.
11.5 Properties

Factors Affecting Resistance

Dependence Factors

  1. Length (l): Directly proportional (R ∝ l).
  2. Area (A): Inversely proportional (R ∝ 1/A).
  3. Temperature: Directly proportional.
  4. Material: Depends on nature of material.
R = ρ (l / A)

Resistivity (ρ)

Resistance offered by a cube of material of side 1m when current flows perpendicular to opposite faces.

  • SI Unit: Ohm-meter (Ωm).
  • Does not change with dimensions, only with material and temperature.
  • Metals/Alloys: Low resistivity (10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ Ωm).
  • Insulators: High resistivity (10¹² to 10¹⁷ Ωm).
  • Alloys don't oxidize vertically at high temps (Used in heaters).
11.6 Combinations

System of Resistors

1. Resistors in Series

Two or more resistors connected end to end.

Derivation:

Total voltage drops is sum of individual drops:

V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

Apply Ohm's Law (V=IR):

IR = IR₁ + IR₂ + IR₃

IR = I(R₁ + R₂ + R₃)

Result:

Rs = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Key Points:

  • Current (I) is SAME in every part.
  • Equivalent resistance is LARGER than largest individual resistance.

2. Resistors in Parallel

Resistors connected between two common points.

Derivation:

Total current is sum of branch currents:

I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃

Apply Ohm's Law (I=V/R):

V/Rp = V/R₁ + V/R₂ + V/R₃

V/Rp = V(1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃)

Result:

1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

Key Points:

  • Voltage (V) is SAME across each resistor.
  • Equivalent resistance is LESS than smallest individual resistance.
Advantages of Parallel Combination:
  1. In series, if one component fails, the circuit is broken and none work. In parallel, others continue to work.
  2. Different appliances have different current requirements. This is satisfied in parallel (current divides).
  3. The total resistance in a parallel circuit is decreased, allowing efficient power usage.
11.7 Thermal Effects

Heating Effect & Applications

Heating Effect of Electric Circuit

If an electric circuit is purely resistive, the source of energy continually gets dissipated entirely in the form of heat.

Heat Produced (Joule's Law) H = I²Rt

Joule's Law of Heating: Heat produced in a resistor is:

  1. Directly proportional to square of current (H ∝ I²).
  2. Directly proportional to resistance (H ∝ R).
  3. Directly proportional to time (H ∝ t).
Desirable Heating Effect

Electric heater, Electric iron, Electric bulb, Electric fuse.

Undesirable Heating Effect

Computers, Monitors (CRT), TV, Refrigerators.

Electric Bulb

Most power consumed by filament appears as heat, small part radiated as light.

  • Filament: Tungsten.
  • (i) Does not oxidize readily at high temp.
  • (ii) High melting point (3380° C).
  • Gas Filling: Inactive gases like Nitrogen and Argon to prolong life.

Electric Fuse

Safety device to protect appliances from short circuit or overloading.

  • Material: Pure tin or alloy of copper and tin (Pb+Sn).
  • Connection: In series with Live wire.
  • Property: Low melting point.
  • Capacity: Slightly higher than appliance rating.
11.8 Energy Rate

Electric Power (P)

The rate at which electric energy is consumed or dissipated in an electric circuit.

P = VI
P = I²R = V²/R
  • SI Unit: Watt (W).
  • 1 watt = 1 volt × 1 ampere.
  • Commercial Unit: kWh.
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ Joules.
  • 1 kWh = 1 Unit of electric energy.
