1. What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electric charge and a form of energy that can be easily converted into other forms, such as light, heat, or mechanical work. On a microscopic level, electricity refers to the movement of electronsâ€”tiny negatively charged particlesâ€”through a conductor. When electrons flow in a particular direction, an electric current is generated.

Electricity is an essential part of our daily lives. It provides power to our homes, schools, workplaces, and industries. Understanding the concept of electricity helps us learn about various devices we use every day and how to use them safely and efficiently.

ðŸ’¡ Key Fact: Electric current is measured in Amperes (A), and it represents the flow of charges through a conductor.

2. Types of Electricity

Electricity can be classified into two main types:

Static Electricity

Static electricity occurs due to an uneven distribution of electric charges on the surface of an object. This leads to effects such as sparks and lightning. Examples include:

  • Lightning during thunderstorms
  • Sparks when removing a sweater
  • Static shock when touching a doorknob
  • Attraction of small paper pieces to a charged comb

Current Electricity

Current electricity is the electricity used to power electrical devices. It involves the continuous flow of electric charges (electrons) through conductors, such as wires and metallic paths.

  • Powers household appliances
  • Used in industrial machinery
  • Lights our homes and streets
  • Powers electronic devices and computers

4. Factors Affecting Resistance

Several factors influence the resistance of a conductor:

1. Material of the Conductor

Different materials have different resistivities. Conductors like copper and aluminium have low resistance and allow current to flow easily. Insulators like rubber and wood have high resistance and prevent current flow.

2. Length of the Conductor (L)

Resistance is directly proportional to the length of the conductor. Longer conductors have higher resistance because electrons have to travel a greater distance and experience more collisions.

âš¡ Remember: If you double the length, resistance doubles.

3. Cross-sectional Area (A)

Resistance is inversely proportional to the conductor's cross-sectional area. A thicker wire has lower resistance because there are more paths for electrons to flow. A thinner wire has higher resistance.

âš¡ Remember: If you double the cross-sectional area, resistance is halved.

4. Temperature

Generally, resistance increases with temperature in metals and semiconductors. At higher temperatures, atoms vibrate more vigorously, causing more collisions with electrons, thus increasing resistance.

Series Connection

Types of Circuits

Series Circuit

In a series circuit, components are connected in a single continuous path. The same current flows through all components.

  • Advantages: Simple design, easy to control
  • Disadvantages: If one component fails, entire circuit stops
  • Uses: Decorative lights (older designs), simple models

Parallel Circuit

In a parallel circuit, components are arranged in multiple independent paths. Each component has the same voltage across it.

  • Advantages: Each appliance works independently, failure of one doesn't affect others
  • Disadvantages: More complex wiring required
  • Uses: Household wiring, electronic devices
