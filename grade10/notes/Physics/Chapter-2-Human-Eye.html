Structure of the Human Eye

The human eye is a complex organ that allows us to see objects around us. It acts like a camera, with the cornea and lens focusing light, and the retina acting as the light-sensitive film.

Parts of the Human Eye

  • Cornea: Transparent front layer that covers iris and pupil. Refracts light and protects eye.
  • Iris: Colored part (pigmented membrane) that controls pupil size.
  • Pupil: Opening through which light enters the eye. Dilates in dim light, contracts in bright light.
  • Lens: Transparent, biconvex structure that focuses light onto retina. Adjusts its focal length (accommodation).
  • Retina: Light-sensitive tissue at back of eye containing rods and cones (photoreceptor cells).
  • Optic Nerve: Transmits visual signals to brain for interpretation.
  • Vitreous Humor: Transparent gel filling eyeball.
  • Aqueous Humor: Clear fluid between cornea and lens.
  • Sclera: White, tough outer layer protecting eye.
  • Ciliary Muscles: Control lens shape for focusing (accommodation).

How the Eye Sees

  1. Light enters through cornea and is refracted
  2. Pupil controls amount of light entering
  3. Lens focuses light onto retina
  4. Retina converts light to electrical signals (photoreceptors: rods for B&W, cones for color)
  5. Optic nerve transmits signals to brain's visual cortex
  6. Brain interprets signals as images

Accommodation

The ability of eye to change focal length to focus on objects at different distances.

  • Near Vision: Ciliary muscles contract, lens becomes thicker, focal length decreases
  • Distance Vision: Ciliary muscles relax, lens becomes thinner, focal length increases
  • Near Point: Closest distance eye can focus clearly (typically 25 cm for normal eye)
  • Far Point: Farthest distance eye can focus clearly (infinity for normal eye)

Defects of Vision

Common vision problems that affect the eye's ability to focus light properly:

  • Myopia (Short-sightedness): Can see nearby objects clearly but distant objects appear blurred
    • Cause: Lens too strong or eyeball too long
    • Light focuses in front of retina
    • Correction: Concave lens (negative power)
  • Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness): Can see distant objects clearly but nearby objects appear blurred
    • Cause: Lens too weak or eyeball too short
    • Light focuses behind retina
    • Correction: Convex lens (positive power)
  • Presbyopia: Age-related gradual loss of eye's accommodation ability
    • Usually starts after age 40
    • Lens loses elasticity
    • Correction: Bifocals or progressive lenses
  • Astigmatism: Cornea or lens is irregularly curved
    • Causes blurred vision at all distances
    • Correction: Cylindrical lens
  • Color Blindness: Inability to distinguish certain colors (usually red-green)
    • Genetic condition
    • Deficiency in cone cells

Dispersion of Light

Dispersion is the phenomenon in which white light is split into its component colors. Different colors have different wavelengths and travel at slightly different speeds in a medium.

The Spectrum: White light disperses into seven colors in order:
Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet (ROYGBIV)

Wavelengths (in vacuum):
Red: 700-650 nm | Orange: 650-590 nm | Yellow: 590-570 nm
Green: 570-495 nm | Blue: 495-450 nm | Indigo: 450-420 nm | Violet: 420-380 nm

Causes of Dispersion

  • Different wavelengths have different refractive indices in a medium
  • Violet has highest refractive index (bends most)
  • Red has lowest refractive index (bends least)

Rainbow Formation

Rainbow is formed when white light from sun undergoes dispersion in water droplets in atmosphere.

  • Sunlight enters water droplet and refracts
  • Light disperses into colors
  • Light reflects at back of droplet
  • Light refracts again as it exits droplet

Why is the Sky Blue?

Blue light has shorter wavelength than red, so it scatters more in atmosphere. Blue light from all directions reaches our eyes, making sky appear blue.

Why is Sunset Red?

At sunset, sunlight travels longer distance through atmosphere. Blue light scatters away, only red and orange light (longer wavelengths) reach observer, making sunset appear red/orange.

Why Do Clouds Appear White?

Cloud droplets are much larger than light wavelength. All colors scatter equally, so clouds appear white.
