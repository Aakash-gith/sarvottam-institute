9.1 Reflection of Light

The Nature of Light

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see. It travels in straight lines (Rectilinear propagation).

  • Speed of Light: 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.
  • Wave-Particle Duality: Light exhibits properties of both waves and particles.

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a polished surface like a mirror.

Laws of Reflection

The laws of reflection apply to all kinds of reflecting surfaces, including spherical surfaces.

  1. The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.
    ∠i = ∠r
  2. The incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray, all lie in the same plane.

9.1.2 Images & Plane Mirrors

Image

It is a point where at least two light rays actually meet or appear to meet.

Real Image Virtual Image
Formed when light rays actually meet. Formed when light rays appear to meet.
Can be obtained on a screen. Can't be obtained on a screen.
Inverted. Erect.
Example: Cinema screen. Example: Plane mirror, Convex mirror.

Image Formed by Plane Mirror

  • Virtual and Erect.
  • Size of image is equal to the size of object.
  • Image is formed as far behind the mirror as the object is in front.
  • Laterally Inverted.
Lateral Inversion: The right side of the object appears left side of the image and vice-versa.

Application: 'AMBULANCE' is written backwards so it reads correctly in rear-view mirrors.

Rules for Ray Diagrams

Standard Rules for Concave Mirrors

Rule 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis will pass through the Principal Focus (F) after reflection.
Rule 2: A ray passing through the Principal Focus (F) will emerge parallel to the principal axis after reflection.
Rule 3: A ray passing through the Center of Curvature (C) is reflected back along the same path.
Rule 4: A ray incident obliquely to the Pole (P) is reflected obliquely making equal angles (∠i = ∠r).

9.2 Spherical Mirrors

Mirror Types

Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved inwards. It converges light rays (Converging Mirror).

Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved outwards. It diverges light rays (Diverging Mirror).

Term Definition
Pole (P) Center of the reflecting surface.
Centre of Curvature (C) Center of the sphere the mirror is part of.
Focus (F) Point where parallel rays meet (concave) or appear to come from (convex).
Focal Length (f) Distance between Pole and Focus. R = 2f

Uses of Concave Mirrors

  • Torches & Headlights: Used to get powerful parallel beams of light.
  • Dentists: Used to see large images of teeth (Object between P & F).
  • Shaving/Makeup: Used to see a larger image of the face.
  • Solar Furnaces: Large concave mirrors concentrate sunlight to produce heat.

Image Formation (Convex Mirror)

Case 1: At Infinity
Image: At F (Behind Mirror)
Size: Point-sized
Case 2: Finite Distance
Image: Between P & F
Size: Diminished

Uses of Convex Mirrors

  • Rear-view Mirrors: Only mirror that gives an erect (though diminished) image. Covers a wider field of view.
  • Security Mirrors: Used in shops to monitor activities.
  • Traffic Mirrors: Used at blind turns to see merging traffic.

Mirror Formula

Relation between u, v, and f:

1/v + 1/u = 1/f
  • u:  Object distance (always -ve)
  • v:  Image distance
  • f:  Focal length

Magnification (m)

Ratio of image height to object height:

m = h'/h = -v/u
  • m ( - ):  Real & Inverted
  • m ( + ):  Virtual & Erect
  • |m| > 1:  Enlarged
  • |m| < 1:  Diminished

Sign Convention (Cartesian)

  • Light travels Left → Right.
  • Origin is at the Pole (P).
  • Distances in direction of light: Positive (+)
  • Distances against light: Negative (-)
  • Height upwards: Positive (+)
  • Height downwards: Negative (-)

9.3 Refraction of Light

Concept

Bending of light when passing obliquely between transparent media.

  • Rarer → Denser: Bends TOWARDS normal (v ↓).
  • Denser → Rarer: Bends AWAY from normal (v ↑).

Glass Slab Refraction

Incident & Emergent rays are parallel but laterally displaced.

Laws of Refraction

  1. Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie in the same plane.
  2. Snell's Law: Ratio of sine of angles is constant for a given pair of media.
sin i / sin r = constant (n₂₁)

Refractive Index (nm)

nm = c / v
c: Speed in Air (3 × 10⁸ m/s)
v: Speed in Medium

9.4 Spherical Lenses

Types of Lenses

Convex Lens: Thicker at middle. Converging.

Concave Lens: Thinner at middle. Diverging.

Visual Trick: A convex lens magnifies text when held close. A concave lens diminishes it.

Lens Formula

1/v - 1/u = 1/f

⚠ Note the negative sign (contrast with mirror).

Magnification (m)

m = h'/h = v/u

Power of Lens (P)

Reciprocal of focal length (in meters). Defines converging/diverging capacity.

P = 1 / f(in meters)
  • SI Unit: Diopter (D)
  • Convex Lens: Positive Power (+D)
  • Concave Lens: Negative Power (-D)
  • Combination: P = P₁ + P₂ + ...
