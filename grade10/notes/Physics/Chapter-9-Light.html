Bending of Light

9.1 Reflection of Light

The Nature of Light

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see. It travels in straight lines (Rectilinear propagation).

  • Speed of Light: 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.
  • Wave-Particle Duality: Light exhibits properties of both waves and particles.

Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it strikes a polished surface like a mirror.

Laws of Reflection

The laws of reflection apply to all kinds of reflecting surfaces, including spherical surfaces.

  1. The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection.
    ∠i = ∠r
  2. The incident ray, the normal to the mirror at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray, all lie in the same plane.

9.1.2 Images & Plane Mirrors

Image

It is a point where at least two light rays actually meet or appear to meet.

Real Image Virtual Image
Formed when light rays actually meet. Formed when light rays appear to meet.
Can be obtained on a screen. Can't be obtained on a screen.
Inverted. Erect.
Example: Cinema screen. Example: Plane mirror, Convex mirror.

Image Formed by Plane Mirror

  • Virtual and Erect.
  • Size of image is equal to the size of object.
  • Image is formed as far behind the mirror as the object is in front.
  • Laterally Inverted.
Lateral Inversion: The right side of the object appears left side of the image and vice-versa.

Application: 'AMBULANCE' is written backwards so it reads correctly in rear-view mirrors.

Rules for Ray Diagrams

Standard Rules for Concave Mirrors

Rule 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis will pass through the Principal Focus (F) after reflection.
Rule 2: A ray passing through the Principal Focus (F) will emerge parallel to the principal axis after reflection.
Rule 3: A ray passing through the Center of Curvature (C) is reflected back along the same path.
Rule 4: A ray incident obliquely to the Pole (P) is reflected obliquely making equal angles (∠i = ∠r).

9.2 Spherical Mirrors

Mirror Types

Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved inwards. It converges light rays (Converging Mirror).

Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface is curved outwards. It diverges light rays (Diverging Mirror).

Term Definition
Pole (P) Center of the reflecting surface.
Centre of Curvature (C) Center of the sphere the mirror is part of.
Focus (F) Point where parallel rays meet (concave) or appear to come from (convex).
Focal Length (f) Distance between Pole and Focus. R = 2f

Image Formation (Concave Mirror)

The nature of the image formed depends on the position of the object.

Uses of Concave Mirrors

  • Torches & Headlights: Used to get powerful parallel beams of light.
  • Dentists: Used to see large images of teeth (Object between P & F).
  • Shaving/Makeup: Used to see a larger image of the face.
  • Solar Furnaces: Large concave mirrors concentrate sunlight to produce heat.

Rules for Image Formation by Convex Mirror

Rule 1: A ray of light parallel to the principal axis appears to diverge from the Principal Focus (F) after reflection.
Rule 2: A ray directed towards the Principal Focus (F) will emerge parallel to the principal axis after reflection.
Rule 3: A ray directed towards the Center of Curvature (C) is reflected back along the same path.
Rule 4: A ray incident obliquely to the Pole (P) is reflected obliquely making equal angles (∠i = ∠r).

Image Formation (Convex Mirror)

Case 1: At Infinity
Image: At F (Behind Mirror)
Size: Point-sized
Case 2: Finite Distance
Image: Between P & F
Size: Diminished

Uses of Convex Mirrors

  1. Convex mirrors are used as rear-view mirrors in vehicles because:
    1. They always give an erect though diminished image.
    2. They have a wider field of view as they are curved outwards.
  2. Used at blind turns and on points of merging traffic to facilitate vision of both side traffic.
  3. Used in shops as security mirrors.

Sign Convention for Reflection by Spherical Mirror

Or New Cartesian Sign Convention

  1. The object is placed to the left of the mirror.
  2. All distances parallel to the principal axis are measured from the pole of the mirror.
  3. All distances measured in the direction of incident ray (along + X-axis) are taken as positive and those measured against the direction of incident ray (along - X-axis) are taken as negative.
  4. Distance measured perpendicular to and above the principal axis are taken as positive.
  5. Distances measured perpendicular to and below the principal axis are taken as negative.
  • Object distance = 'u' is always negative.
  • Focal length of concave mirror = Negative (f = -ve)
  • Focal length of convex mirror = Positive (f = +ve)

Mirror Formula

Relation between u, v, and f:

1/v + 1/u = 1/f
  • u: Object distance (always -ve)
  • v: Image distance
  • f: Focal length

Magnification (m)

Ratio of image height to object height:

m = h'/h = -v/u
  • m ( - ): Real & Inverted
  • m ( + ): Virtual & Erect
  • |m| > 1: Enlarged
  • |m| < 1: Diminished

Sign Convention (Cartesian)

  • Light travels Left → Right.
  • Origin is at the Pole (P).
  • Distances in direction of light: Positive (+)
  • Distances against light: Negative (-)
  • Height upwards: Positive (+)
  • Height downwards: Negative (-)

9.3 Refraction of Light

Refraction of Light

Bending of light when it enters obliquely from one transparent medium to another.

  • Speed of light: Maximum in vacuum (3 × 10⁸ m/s).
  • Cause of Refraction: Change in speed of light.

Examples:

  • The bottom of a swimming pool appears higher.
  • A pencil partially immersed in water appears to be bent.
  • Lemons in a glass tumbler appear bigger.
  • Letters appear raised through a glass slab.

Optically Mediums

Optically rarer medium: Out of two given media, the medium with lower value of refractive index.

Rarer → Denser

Light bends towards the normal.

Denser → Rarer

Light bends away from the normal.

* Refractive index of a medium does not depend on physical density.

Refraction through Glass Slab

Incident & Emergent rays are parallel but laterally displaced.

Lateral Displacement depends on:

  • Refractive index of glass slab
  • Thickness of glass slab
  • Wavelength of light rays
  • Angle of incidence

Laws of Refraction

  1. The incident ray, the refracted ray and the normal to the interface of two transparent media at the point of incidence, all lie in the same plane.
  2. Snell's Law: The ratio of sine of angle of incidence to the sine of angle of refraction is a constant.
sin i / sin r = constant

Refractive Index (n)

The ratio of speed of light in a given pair of media.

Formula:
n = (Velocity of light in medium 1) / (Velocity of light in medium 2)
  • n₂₁: Refractive index of medium 2 w.r.t medium 1
    n₂₁ = v₁ / v₂
  • n₁₂: Refractive index of medium 1 w.r.t medium 2
    n₁₂ = v₂ / v₁

Key Relations

Absolute Refractive Index

Refractive index of a medium w.r.t vacuum or air.

n = c / v
(c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
  • Reciprocal Relation:
    Refractive index of one medium is reciprocal of other's in a given pair.
    n₁₂ = 1 / n₂₁
  • Relative Formula:
    n₂₁ = n₂ / n₁ (where n is absolute RI)
Example: Diamond
Refractive index is 2.42 (highest).
It means speed of light is 1/2.42 times in diamond of that in vacuum.

9.4 Spherical Lenses

Spherical Lenses

A transparent medium bound by two surfaces, of which one or both surfaces are curved.

Convex Lens

  • Thin from corners
  • Thick at center
  • Converging

Concave Lens

  • Thick from corners
  • Thin at center
  • Diverging

Rules for Image Formation by Convex Lens

Rule 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis passes through the focus (F₂) on the other side.
Rule 2: A ray passing through the principal focus (F₁) emerges parallel to the principal axis.
Rule 3: A ray passing through the Optical Center (O) emerges without any deviation.

Sign convention for spherical lenses

  • Sign conventions are similar to the one used for spherical mirrors, except that measurements are taken from optical center of the lens.
  • Focal length of convex lens = Positive
  • Focal length of concave lens = Negative

Lens Formula

1/v - 1/u = 1/f

Magnification

m = hᵢ/hₒ
Also, m = v/u
∴ hᵢ = height of image, hₒ = height of object

Power of Lens (P)

It is defined as the reciprocal of focal length in meter.
The degree of convergence or divergence of light rays is expressed in terms of power.

Power = 1 / focal length (in meter)    P = 1/f
  • SI Unit of Power: dioptre = D
  • 1 D = 1 m⁻¹
    1 dioptre is the power of lens whose focal length is one meter.
  • Power of convex lens: Positive
  • Power of concave lens: Negative
  • Power of a lens combination:
    P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃.........
