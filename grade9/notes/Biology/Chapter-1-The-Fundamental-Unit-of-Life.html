Introduction

What is a Cell?

Just like bricks make a building, cells make up every living organism.

Definition

The cell is the structural and functional unit of life. All living organisms are composed of cells.

  • Structural: Provides structure to the body.
  • Functional: Performs all vital functions (respiration, nutrition, etc.).

Key Characteristics

Cells are capable of independent existence and perform all essential functions of life. Anything less than a complete structure of a cell does not ensure independent living.

How did we find cells?

History & Cell Theory

Timeline of major discoveries that shaped modern cell biology.

Discovery of the Cell

The science of cells began in 1665 when Robert Hooke used a simple microscope to observe a thin slice of cork. He saw many tiny box-like compartments, which reminded him of small rooms called ‚Äúcells", and the name remained.

  • 1665 ‚Äì Hooke describes cork cells.
  • 1838 ‚Äì Schleiden: all plants are made of cells.
  • 1839 ‚Äì Schwann: all animals are made of cells.
  • 1858 ‚Äì Virchow: new cells arise from pre-existing cells.
Try this Q: Why did Hooke see only dead cells in cork and not living ones?

Modern Cell Theory

Today, the modern cell theory extends these ideas and is often summarised in three powerful statements:

  • Cell is the smallest structural unit of all living beings.
  • Cell is also the functional unit ‚Äì all life processes occur in cells.
  • Every cell originates from a pre-existing cell by cell division.
Exam tip Virchow = ‚Äúcells from cells‚Äù. This line is commonly asked in short-answer questions.
Variety of cells

Size, Shape & Number of Cells

One chapter, but infinite shapes‚Äîfrom bacteria to ostrich eggs.

Number of Cells

Living organisms may be made of a single cell or millions of cells:

  • Unicellular ‚Äì Amoeba, Paramecium.
  • Few-celled ‚Äì simple algae like Spirogyra.
  • Multicellular ‚Äì humans, plants, animals.
Think Q: In a multicellular organism, can a single cell survive on its own? Why/why not?

Cell Size

Cells show wide size differences:

  • Smallest ‚Äì many bacteria (microscopic).
  • Longest ‚Äì nerve cells in animals.
  • Largest single cell ‚Äì ostrich egg.
Memory tip Longest cell ‚Üí nerve cell. Largest cell ‚Üí ostrich egg.

Cell Shape

Cell shape is closely linked to its function:

  • Red blood cells ‚Äì biconcave disc for easy gas exchange.
  • Nerve cells ‚Äì long and branched to transmit impulses.
  • Columnar epithelial cells ‚Äì tall, for absorption (intestine).
  • Amoeba, WBC ‚Äì change shape to move and engulf particles.
Big classification

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

Ancient minimalists versus modern specialists.

Prokaryotic Cells

Prokaryotes (pro = ‚Äúbefore", karyon = ‚Äúnucleus") are ancient, simple cells whose DNA is not enclosed in a true nucleus.

  • No well-defined nuclear membrane; DNA lies naked.
  • No membrane-bound organelles (no mitochondria, Golgi, etc.).
  • Have cell wall, plasma membrane and cytoplasm.
  • Contain ribosomes for protein synthesis.
  • May have plasmids (extra circular DNA).
  • Mesosomes help in cellular respiration.
  • Flagella and pili help in movement and attachment.

Eukaryotic Cells

Eukaryotes (eu = ‚Äútrue") possess a true nucleus and a full set of membrane-bound organelles, making them structurally complex and efficient.

  • DNA enclosed within a double-layered nuclear membrane.
  • Have organelles like mitochondria, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, plastids, etc.
  • Cell wall present in plant cells, absent in animals.
  • Chloroplasts in plants carry out photosynthesis.
Memory tip You ‚Äúcarry‚Äù a nucleus ‚Üí Eukaryote. No nucleus wall ‚Üí Prokaryote.
Inside the cell

Cell Organelles & Their Functions

Think of a cell as a tiny city, with each organelle doing a special job.

1. Cell Membrane

The thin, living boundary of the cell made of a phospholipid bilayer and proteins. It is selectively permeable, allowing only certain substances in or out.

  • Maintains internal environment of the cell.
  • Controls entry and exit of ions, nutrients and waste.
  • Site for processes like diffusion, osmosis and active transport.
Exam tip If the question says ‚Äúfluid mosaic model‚Äù, they‚Äôre talking about the cell membrane.

2. Cell Wall (Plants)

A rigid outer covering made of cellulose that lies outside the cell membrane in plants, fungi and some bacteria.

  • Provides definite shape and mechanical strength.
  • Prevents bursting in hypotonic environments.
  • Freely permeable to water and small molecules.

3. Cytoplasm

Semi-fluid, jelly-like substance filling the inside of the cell. All organelles are suspended in it.

  • Site where many metabolic reactions occur (e.g. glycolysis).
  • Distributes materials throughout the cell.

4. Nucleus

The control centre of the cell. It is a double-membrane-bound organelle containing the cell‚Äôs genetic material.

  • Nuclear membrane with pores controls exchange with cytoplasm.
  • Chromatin (DNA + protein) carries genes.
  • Condenses into chromosomes during cell division.
  • Nucleolus inside nucleus synthesises rRNA and forms ribosomes.
Check Q: Why is the nucleus called the ‚Äúdirector‚Äù or ‚Äúbrain‚Äù of the cell?

5. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

Rough ER (RER)

  • Studded with ribosomes ‚Üí rough appearance.
  • Synthesises and modifies proteins.
  • Transports proteins to other regions, including Golgi apparatus.

Smooth ER (SER)

  • No ribosomes ‚Üí smooth.
  • Synthesises lipids and steroids.
  • Detoxifies drugs and poisonous substances in liver cells.
Quick rule Proteins ‚Üí RER ¬∑ Fats & detox ‚Üí SER.

6. Golgi Apparatus

A stack of membrane-bound sacs and vesicles. Often called the ‚Äúpost office‚Äù or ‚Äúpackaging centre‚Äù of the cell.

  • Modifies, sorts and packages proteins and lipids.
  • Forms secretory vesicles and lysosomes.
  • Important for synthesis of cell wall components in plants.

7. Lysosomes

Membrane-bound sacs filled with digestive enzymes. They break down unwanted materials.

  • Digest worn-out organelles, food particles and engulfed bacteria.
  • Can digest the entire cell when it is damaged or old ‚Üí ‚Äúsuicide bags‚Äù.
Suicide bags Digestive bags Demolition squad

8. Mitochondria

Double-membraned organelles often called the ‚Äúpowerhouse of the cell‚Äù.

  • Inner membrane forms folds called cristae for increased surface area.
  • Site of aerobic respiration.
  • Produce ATP, the energy currency of the cell.
  • Contain their own DNA and ribosomes ‚Üí semi-autonomous.
Exam tip Any question involving ATP or ‚Äúenergy‚Äù is almost always pointing to mitochondria.

9. Plastids (Plant Cells)

Plastids are double-membrane organelles found only in plant cells.

  • Chloroplasts ‚Äì green pigment (chlorophyll), site of photosynthesis.
  • Chromoplasts ‚Äì coloured plastids, give flowers and fruits their colour.
  • Leucoplasts ‚Äì colourless, store starch, oil and proteins.
Try this Q: Which plastid would be abundant in potato tubers and in petals of a red flower?

10. Vacuoles

Large, fluid-filled sacs surrounded by a single membrane (tonoplast), especially prominent in plant cells.

  • Store water, food, pigments and waste products.
  • Provide turgidity and rigidity to plant cells.
  • In Amoeba, contractile vacuoles help in osmoregulation.

11. Centrosome & Centrioles (Animals)

Present only in animal cells, the centrosome contains a pair of centrioles surrounded by microtubules.

  • Play a key role in spindle formation during cell division.
  • Help in equal distribution of chromosomes to daughter cells.

Transport of Materials

How substances move in and out: diffusion, osmosis, and more.

Passive Transport

Movement of substances without using cellular energy (ATP) from higher to lower concentration.

  • Simple diffusion ‚Äì gases like O‚ÇÇ and CO‚ÇÇ move directly through the membrane.
  • Facilitated diffusion ‚Äì ions or molecules use carrier proteins (permeases).
  • Osmosis ‚Äì movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane.
Check Q: Why is osmosis called a special case of diffusion?

Osmosis in Detail

  • Endosmosis ‚Äì water enters the cell; cell swells.
  • Exosmosis ‚Äì water leaves the cell; cell shrinks.
  • Plasmolysis ‚Äì in plant cells, cytoplasm and membrane shrink away from cell wall due to exosmosis.

Depending on surrounding solution:

  • Isotonic ‚Äì equal solute; no net water movement.
  • Hypotonic ‚Äì less solute outside; water enters; cell may swell.
  • Hypertonic ‚Äì more solute outside; water leaves; cell shrinks.
Memory tip Hyper = ‚Äúmore‚Äù solute outside ‚Üí cell loses water and shrinks.

Active Transport

Movement of substances from lower to higher concentration using energy (ATP).

  • Always against the concentration gradient.
  • Requires specific carrier proteins in the membrane.

Bulk Transport

Movement of large particles or large volumes of fluid by forming vesicles.

  • Endocytosis ‚Äì materials move into the cell.
  •   ‚Ä¢ Phagocytosis ‚Äì ‚Äúcell eating‚Äù, intake of solids.
  •   ‚Ä¢ Pinocytosis ‚Äì ‚Äúcell drinking‚Äù, intake of fluid.
  •   ‚Ä¢ Receptor-mediated endocytosis ‚Äì highly specific uptake.
  • Exocytosis ‚Äì vesicles fuse with membrane to release contents outside.
Example Amoeba engulfing food using pseudopodia is a classic case of endocytosis.

Cellulose & Digestion

Why humans eat fibre but can‚Äôt digest it.

What is Cellulose?

Cellulose is a complex carbohydrate (polysaccharide) made of long chains of glucose molecules linked by special Œ≤-glycosidic bonds.

  • Main component of plant cell walls.
  • Insoluble in water and chemically inert in pure form.
  • Gives plants strength and rigidity.

Digestion of Cellulose

  • Termites ‚Äì contain microbes in their gut that produce enzymes to digest cellulose.
  • Ruminants (cow, buffalo) ‚Äì have a rumen with bacteria that digest cellulose anaerobically, releasing methane.
  • Humans ‚Äì lack the enzyme to break Œ≤-linkages, so cellulose is not digested.

In humans, cellulose acts as dietary fibre or roughage. It:

  • adds bulk to food,
  • helps movement of food in intestine,
  • reduces risk of constipation and colon problems.

1. Why is the cell called the ‚Äústructural and functional unit of life‚Äù?
+
Because every living organism is made of cells (structural), and all life processes such as respiration, nutrition, excretion, and reproduction occur within cells (functional).
2. State two differences between diffusion and osmosis.
+
  • Diffusion can involve any particle (gas/solute), whereas osmosis involves only water.
  • Osmosis requires a selectively permeable membrane; diffusion does not.
3. Name two cell organelles having their own DNA.
+
Mitochondria and plastids (especially chloroplasts) have their own DNA and ribosomes, so they are semi-autonomous.
4. Why don‚Äôt human beings digest cellulose like cows?
+
Humans lack the specific enzyme needed to break the Œ≤-glycosidic linkages in cellulose. Ruminants have symbiotic bacteria in the rumen that produce this enzyme.
5. What will happen to a plant cell placed in a hypertonic solution?
+
Water will leave the cell by exosmosis. The cytoplasm and cell membrane shrink away from the cell wall, and the cell becomes plasmolysed.
